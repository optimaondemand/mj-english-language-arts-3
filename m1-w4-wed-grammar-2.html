Lesson 1.18
All the Parts at Once
Grammar Work Session
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You have learned the parts one at a time. Today they all show up in the same sentence.
Over the last weeks you met the eight kinds of words that build every sentence: nouns (names of people and things), pronouns (words that stand in for nouns: she, it, they), verbs (action or linking), adjectives and articles (the, a, an), adverbs (how, when, where, how much), prepositions (position words), conjunctions (joining words: and, but, or), and interjections (sudden words: oh, alas). Today is a work session: you will take a real sentence from Emma and decide what job each word is doing. Naming the parts is not the goal in itself; it is how you slow down enough to see exactly how Austen built the line.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to name the part of speech for each word in a short sentence.
2
I will be able to use the same word’s job to read a sentence more closely.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which feels harder when a sentence is long? (No wrong answer.)

Here is one short line from Emma, with every word labeled. Read it slowly and watch how the labels fit together.

“Emma walked quietly into the room.”
Emma = noun (a name) · walked = verb (action) · quietly = adverb (how she walked)
into = preposition (position) · the = article · room = noun (object of the preposition)
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN ON AN EMMA SENTENCE

Work through this line word by word: “Frank suddenly arrived and charmed everyone.” Choose the job of each named word.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME EACH WORD’S JOB
In “Frank suddenly arrived and charmed everyone,” what is each word?
1. The word “Frank” is a…
2. The word “suddenly” is an…
3. The word “and” is a…
4. The word “charmed” is a…
✏️ PART 3 · FULL WORK SESSION

Now do the whole thing yourself in your notebook. Copy this line from Emma and write the part of speech above each word.

“Oh! she was a charming young woman, and Mr. Weston liked her exceedingly.”
▶  Label every word first. Then click to check your work.
Oh = interjection · she = pronoun · was = linking verb · a = article · charming = adjective · young = adjective · woman = noun · and = conjunction · Mr. Weston = noun · liked = action verb · her = pronoun · exceedingly = adverb. (Check yours against this. Where you disagreed, ask what job the word does in the sentence.)
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Naming the parts is a way of seeing. When you notice that Austen piles two adjectives before a noun, or drops in an interjection, you are noticing a choice she made. You will use exactly this skill in your Parts of Speech Project. Memory work: drill all your memorized passages this week.
