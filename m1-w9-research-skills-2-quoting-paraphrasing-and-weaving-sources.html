📖 PART 1 · THREE WAYS TO USE A SOURCE

There are three honest ways to bring someone else’s words or ideas into your own writing, and all three require a citation. A direct quotation uses the source’s exact words, inside quotation marks, because you are borrowing the sentence itself. A paraphrase restates the idea in your own words and sentence structure, usually about the same length as the original, because you want the specific point without the original phrasing. A summary condenses a longer passage, a whole paragraph or more, down to its main point in a sentence or two of your own. All three still need a citation: a paraphrase or summary that leaves out the source is still using someone else’s idea without credit, even though none of their exact words appear. MLA style (Modern Language Association style) handles this with a short in-text citation in parentheses, usually the author’s last name and a page number, placed right after the borrowed material: for example, (Austen 112), or, for a source with no page numbers, just (Austen).

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
paraphrase
Paraphrase means restating someone else’s idea in your own words and sentence structure, at roughly the same length as the original, while still citing where the idea came from. Changing only a word or two of the original is not a paraphrase; it is too close to the source’s own wording to count as your own.
⚡ PART 2 · WEAVING A SOURCE INTO YOUR PARAGRAPH

Dropping a quotation into a paragraph by itself, with nothing around it, leaves your reader to do the work of connecting it to your point. A well-built paragraph does that work for the reader, in three moves.

1
Introduce the source: name the author or the kind of source, so your reader knows where this evidence is coming from.
2
Give the evidence: the quotation, paraphrase, or summary, cited correctly.
3
Explain, in your own words, what this evidence shows and why it matters to your point. Skipping this move is the most common mistake: a quotation that just sits there, unexplained, does not prove anything by itself. It is your job to say what it proves.
📖 A WORKED EXAMPLE
One critic argues that Austen’s narration constantly lets Emma misjudge people while quietly showing the reader the truth (Reeve 47). This gap between what Emma believes and what the reader can already see is exactly what makes her mistake at Box Hill so uncomfortable to watch: we know better before she does.

Notice the three moves: the first sentence introduces the critic and gives the cited claim; the second sentence is the writer’s own explanation of why that claim matters.
