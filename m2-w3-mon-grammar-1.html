📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A phrase is a small team of words that works together without a subject and a verb of its own.
Lewis builds his strange world out of careful detail, and much of that detail rides on phrases. A prepositional phrase begins with a preposition (a word like in, on, under, beyond, through, across) and ends with a noun, telling you where, when, or how: across the pale water, under a copper sky. An appositive phrase sits right beside a noun and renames it, adding a quick burst of information: Ransom, a philologist on a walking tour, noticed the smell first. The appositive is the part set off by commas. Learning to spot both lets you read long sentences with confidence and write your own world with the same precision.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find a prepositional phrase and tell what it adds to a sentence.
2
I will be able to recognize an appositive phrase and explain the noun it renames.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which of these tells you more about where something is happening? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the quick test. A prepositional phrase usually answers where, when, or how. An appositive phrase usually answers which one or what kind, and you can lift it out, commas and all, and the sentence still stands.

“Ransom climbed through the forest of tall stalks.” → where? Prepositional phrase: through the forest of tall stalks.
“The hross, a tall furred creature, beckoned him.” → renames the hross. Appositive phrase: a tall furred creature.
“At dawn the water shone like glass.” → when? Prepositional phrase: at dawn.
