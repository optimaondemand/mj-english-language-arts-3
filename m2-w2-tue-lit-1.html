📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A writer controls two feelings at once: the attitude in the words, and the atmosphere in the room.
Two words help you describe how a passage feels. Tone is the writer’s or narrator’s attitude toward the subject, the feeling you hear in the choice of words: wondering, uneasy, mocking, tender. Mood is the atmosphere the passage creates in you, the reader: dread, calm, awe. They often work together, but they can pull apart, and skilled writers shift them on purpose. In Chapters 5 to 8, Ransom is carried through space toward a world he does not understand. Watch how Lewis moves you from terror to a strange, reluctant wonder as the voyage goes on. The feeling does not stay still.

📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN FEEL FOR THE SHIFT
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN

Read Chapters 5 to 8 in your own copy. As you read, mark in your notebook one place where the feeling is fearful and one place where it turns toward wonder.

📖 YOUR READING
Read Chapters 5 to 8 in your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet. This book is still under copyright, so the text is not reproduced here; keep your book beside you for the practice below.

Here is how the two words work, using sentences written for this lesson rather than from the book:

“The metal walls pressed close, and every breath sounded too loud.”
Tone: tense, watchful.   Mood it stirs in you: dread.

“Then the dark thinned, and a light unlike any sunrise washed the cabin gold.”
Tone: awed, softening.   Mood it stirs in you: wonder.

Lewis himself describes the space around the ship not as cold emptiness but, in one short phrase, as a place of "tingling and rejoicing" radiance (Ch. 5). Notice how that single choice of words tilts both the tone and the mood toward wonder. Find the sentence in your own copy and read the whole moment around it.

📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

In these chapters Ransom is filled with foreboding. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
foreboding
Foreboding is a heavy sense that something bad is coming, a dread you feel before you have any proof. It is a feeling word, perfect for describing mood.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Foreboding is native Old English, not Latin or Greek: fore- (“before”) plus bodian (“to announce or foretell”). Plenty of everyday English words come from Old English rather than the classical languages; you can still spot that same fore- in forecast and foresee.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence using foreboding to describe a mood. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“a foreboding that…”).
