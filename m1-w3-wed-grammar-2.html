Lesson 1.13
How Sentences Are Built
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Most sentences are built from a few simple shapes.
The simplest sentence has just two parts: a subject (who or what the sentence is about) and a verb (what the subject does). Emma smiled. That is the first pattern. The second pattern adds a direct object: the thing that receives the action of the verb. To find a direct object, say the subject and verb, then ask whom? or what? Emma painted Harriet. Painted whom? Harriet. So Harriet is the direct object. Learning these two shapes lets you see the bones of any sentence, even a long Austen one, by stripping it down to its subject, its verb, and whatever the verb acts on.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell a subject-verb sentence from a subject-verb-object sentence.
2
I will be able to find the direct object by asking “the verb whom or what?”
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which sentence has something that receives the action? (No wrong answer.)

First find the subject and verb. Then ask the verb “whom?” or “what?” If there is an answer, that answer is the direct object and you have the second pattern.

“Harriet hesitated.” → subject Harriet, verb hesitated. Hesitated what? Nothing. Subject + verb.
“Emma made a plan.” → subject Emma, verb made. Made what? a plan. Subject + verb + direct object.
“Mr. Elton wrote a riddle.” → wrote what? a riddle. Subject + verb + direct object.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Which pattern does each sentence follow?

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH PATTERN?
Choose the pattern of each sentence.
1. Emma drew a portrait.
2. Mr. Elton laughed.
3. Emma refused his offer.
4. Harriet waited patiently.
▶  In your notebook, write one sentence in each pattern: one with just a subject and verb, one that adds a direct object. Then click to see examples.
Subject + verb: “Emma blushed.”   Subject + verb + object: “Emma kept the secret.” (Kept what? the secret.) Your sentences will differ; check that the object answers “the verb whom or what?”
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Every sentence has a subject and a verb; some add a direct object. When an Austen sentence runs long, find its subject and verb first, then ask whether the verb acts on anything. Memory work: drill the opening sentence, and keep working on “It was badly done, indeed!”
