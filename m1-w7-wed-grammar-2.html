Lesson 1.33
Making the Subject and Verb Agree
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A verb has to match the subject doing it: one or many.
A verb must agree with its subject in number. A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb. So we say “Emma walks” (one person) but “the guests walk” (many). The tricky part is that writers often drop extra words between the subject and the verb, and those words can fool the ear. In “the box of letters sits on the desk,” the verb agrees with box (one box), not with letters. To get agreement right, find the true subject first, ignore the words in between, then ask: one, or more than one?
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find the true subject of a sentence and tell if it is singular or plural.
2
I will be able to choose the verb that agrees, even when words come between subject and verb.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which sounds right to your ear? (No wrong answer; trust your instinct, then we will explain it.)

Here is the move that fixes most agreement errors: cross out the words between the subject and the verb, then check the match.

“The box of letters sits on the desk.” → subject is box (one). Singular verb sits. Correct.
“The Bates ladies live nearby.” → subject is ladies (more than one). Plural verb live. Correct.
“Each of the guests was invited.” → subject is each (treated as one). Singular verb was. Correct.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Choose the verb that agrees with the true subject.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH VERB AGREES?

1. The basket of apples ____ on the table.
2. Emma and Harriet ____ to Highbury together.
3. Each of the sisters ____ an answer.
4. The letters from Miss Bates ____ every week.
▶  In your notebook, write one sentence with words between the subject and the verb, and make them agree. Then click to see an example.
“The crowd of visitors fills the parlor.” The subject is “crowd” (one), so the singular verb “fills” agrees, even with “visitors” nearby. (Your sentence will differ. Cross out the in-between words and check the match.)
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Find the true subject, ignore the words in between, then match one to one or many to many. This week, when you write about the novel, read your sentences back and check that each verb agrees. Memory work: drill all three passages so you can recite each one cleanly.
