Lesson 2.21
Clauses That Can Stand Alone, and Clauses That Cannot
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Every clause has a subject and a verb. Only some can stand on their own.
A clause is a group of words with both a subject and a verb. An independent clause expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence: Ransom listened. A dependent clause also has a subject and a verb, but it cannot stand alone; it leaves you waiting for more: when Ransom listened. Words like when, because, although, if, since, and while open a dependent clause and signal that it needs an independent clause to lean on. Learning to tell the two apart lets you join ideas on purpose instead of by accident, which matters when you are building tension in a scene of your own.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell an independent clause from a dependent clause.
2
I will be able to spot the word that turns an independent clause into a dependent one.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which of these feels finished, like you could stop there? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the test: read the clause by itself. If it stands on its own as a complete thought, it is independent. If a word at the front leaves you waiting for the rest, it is dependent.

“The hrossa sang at the water.” → a complete thought; it can stop here. Independent.
“While the hrossa sang at the water…” → the word “while” leaves you waiting. Dependent.
“Ransom understood them at last.” → complete; it can stand alone. Independent.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Read each clause by itself. Is it independent or dependent?

⚡ TRY IT · INDEPENDENT OR DEPENDENT?
Choose the kind of clause.
1. Ransom heard a strange music.
2. Although the planet frightened him at first.
3. The Oyarsa spoke quietly.
4. Because the men from Earth did not understand him.
▶  In your notebook, write one independent clause about a scene from your own story, then add a word like “when” or “because” to the front to make it dependent. Then click to see an example.
Independent: “The traveler stepped onto the cold sand.”   Dependent: “When the traveler stepped onto the cold sand…” Notice how the dependent version now needs a second clause to finish the idea.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

An independent clause stands alone; a dependent clause leans on it. When you draft your scene this week, you can hold a reader in suspense by opening with a dependent clause and finishing with an independent one. Memory work: begin learning passage 2, the Oyarsa’s judgment on the bent race.
