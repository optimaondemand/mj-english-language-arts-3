Spotting the Reasoning: Study or Wonder?
A short, ungraded page, lighter than usual since this is a one-week module. It sits alongside this week’s grammar and Word Study, not in place of them.
📖 WHY THIS MATTERS
Whitman’s poem only reports one person’s one night: a lecture that wears him out, and a walk outside that does not. It is easy to turn that single moment into a bigger rule than the poem actually makes. Two ways that happens have names.
⚡ ONE · FALSE DILEMMA
A false dilemma claims there are only two choices when other options actually exist. Someone might argue that you must pick one path forever: study the stars scientifically, or feel real wonder at them, never both. Plenty of astronomers feel wonder while doing careful science. The poem describes one person’s one night, not a permanent either/or rule for everybody.
⚡ TRY IT · FALSE DILEMMA, OR JUST WHAT HAPPENED?
For each claim, decide whether it forces a choice between only two options, or simply describes what happened in the poem.
1. A classmate says the poem proves you can either study the stars scientifically or feel real wonder at them, never both.
2. The speaker in the poem grows tired of the lecture, rises, and walks outside to look at the stars in silence.
3. A student argues that everyone must pick one path for life: become a scientist who only measures things, or a dreamer who never studies facts.
⚡ TWO · APPEAL TO AUTHORITY
An appeal to authority treats a claim as settled just because an expert said so, without asking whether the expert’s knowledge actually covers the question at hand. Experts are usually worth listening to on their own subject. The astronomer in the poem is a real expert on facts about the stars. That does not make him an expert on the separate question of how a person should feel while looking at them.
⚡ TRY IT · APPEAL TO AUTHORITY, OR A FAIR USE OF EXPERTISE?
For each claim, decide whether it leans on the astronomer’s expertise where it actually applies, or stretches it to settle a question it cannot answer.
1. Because the astronomer lectures “with much applause,” a listener assumes his way of explaining the stars must be the only valid way to encounter them.
2. If you want to know the distance to a star or how it was formed, it makes sense to trust the astronomer’s measurements over a guess.
3. A student insists that since the lecturer is the expert in the room, anyone who prefers to look at the stars in silence instead of studying his charts must be wrong.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

The poem does not actually argue that science and wonder are enemies. It just shows one tired man choosing silence for one night. Keep that distinction in mind as you write your own Juxtaposition Poem this week.
