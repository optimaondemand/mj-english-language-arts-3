📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A draft becomes a story when you read it as a stranger would.
Across this module each Quick Write added one rung to a ladder: an arrival, a strange landscape, a first meeting, a moment of misunderstanding. Today you turn that ladder into one story. The way Lewis makes his planet feel real is not magic; he gives you what a body would actually notice, in order, so the reader builds the place alongside the traveler. Finish the novel and the Postscript in your own copy this week, then read your own draft slowly, the way a reader who knows nothing about your world would, and ask where they would get lost. That is what revision and peer review are for.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to assemble my Quick Write pieces into one connected story.
2
I will be able to give and use specific peer-review feedback to revise my draft.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your story draft and your earlier Quick Writes, your copy of the novel for the final chapters and Postscript, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · FINISH THE NOVEL, THEN READ YOUR OWN DRAFT
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you reread your own draft, what do you tend to notice first? (No wrong answer.)

Finish Chapters 21 to the end and the Postscript in your own copy. Notice how Lewis lets the narrator step back at the close and reflect on what the journey meant. In the Postscript, Lewis even calls the book, in his own words, an attempt to give readers what he once called “the strangeness” of another world. That is exactly the effect your story is reaching for.

📖 YOUR READING
Read Chapters 21 to the end and the Postscript in your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet. The novel is in copyright, so it is not reproduced here; use your own book.
📝 PART 2 · WORD STUDY

Lewis’s narrator often gives us his first impression of a thing before he understands it. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
impression
Impression means the effect something has on your mind or senses at first, before you fully understand it. In a first-contact story, the early impression is everything: the reader meets the strange world through what the traveler senses before he can name or explain it.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Impression comes from Latin in- (“into”) and premere (“to press”): literally a mark pressed into something. The same root shows up in compress (to press together) and express (to press outward, to make known).
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence using impression about the first thing your traveler senses. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“an impression so strong that…”).
⚡ PART 3 · PRACTICE GOOD FEEDBACK

Helpful peer feedback is specific and points to the text. For each comment a reviewer might leave, choose the most useful one.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH COMMENT HELPS THE WRITER?
Choose the feedback that would actually help a writer revise.
1. On a vague opening paragraph.
2. On a sudden jump between two events.
3. On a strong sensory line.
▶  In your notebook, write the one revision you most need to make before Friday. Click to see how to phrase a clear revision goal.
A clear goal names a place and a change: “Add three lines of sound and smell to the landing in paragraph 1.” or “Connect the cave and the river so the reader knows why he moves.” Yours will be specific to your story.
