📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Three small tools let a writer say more than the words alone.
Today you will name and use three of a writer’s most common tools. A simile compares two things using like or as: “her chatter was like a kettle that would not stop.” A metaphor makes the same kind of comparison without like or as, by saying one thing simply is another: “her chatter was a kettle that would not stop.” And irony happens when the words say one thing but mean the opposite: when the narrator calls Mrs. Elton’s manners “elegant,” we are meant to hear that they are not. Austen leans hardest on irony, because Mrs. Elton constantly says the opposite of the truth about herself. Naming these tools lets you point to exactly how a sentence does its work.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell a simile from a metaphor from an example of irony.
2
I will be able to point to one of each in the writing about Mrs. Elton.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your copy of Emma (or the linked PDF), Vol. II, Ch. 13 to 18, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · THREE TOOLS, ONE WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If a friend says “Oh, lovely” after spilling juice on the floor, what do they actually mean? (No wrong answer.)
Simile: “Mrs. Elton swept in like a queen visiting her subjects.” → uses like.
Metaphor: “In her own mind, Mrs. Elton was the queen of Highbury.” → no like; she simply ‘is.’
Irony: the narrator calls her welcome “all gracious condescension” → the words flatter, but we hear the opposite.
⚡ PART 2 · NAME THE TOOL

Which tool is each sentence using?

⚡ TRY IT · SIMILE, METAPHOR, OR IRONY?
Choose the tool at work in each line.
1. Her voice carried across the room like a trumpet.
2. To Mrs. Elton, her own opinion was law.
3. The narrator describes her endless boasting as “modest.”
4. Mrs. Elton was as busy arranging others as a bee in a garden.
⚡ PART 3 · APPLY IT YOURSELF

Now make your own. Use Mrs. Elton as your subject.

▶  In your notebook, write one simile, one metaphor, and one ironic sentence about Mrs. Elton. Then click to compare with examples.
Simile: “Mrs. Elton clung to the word ‘Maple Grove’ like a child to a favorite toy.”   Metaphor: “In Highbury, Mrs. Elton was a small storm of opinions.”   Irony: “She was, of course, far too humble to mention her sister’s estate more than once a minute.” Your sentences will differ. Check that the simile uses “like” or “as,” the metaphor does not, and the ironic line says the opposite of the truth.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Simile compares with “like” or “as”; metaphor compares without them; irony says the opposite of what it means. Austen’s irony is why Mrs. Elton sounds so sure and looks so foolish. Memory work: drill all three passages so you can recite each one cleanly.
