Lesson 1.34
Austen at Play: Reading Her Comic Verse Aloud
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The same writer can sound completely different in verse than in prose.
Austen is famous for her novels, but she also wrote light, funny verse for family and friends. The poem you will read today was written to tease a Mr. Best who would not travel to Harrogate. It is fast, rhyming, and openly silly. Her prose in Emma is something else entirely: long, balanced sentences that watch a character so closely you forget the writer is there. Reading the verse aloud lets you hear how a writer’s voice changes with the form. The poem rhymes and bounces; the prose glides and observes. Same wit underneath, two very different sounds on the surface.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to read the verse aloud and hear its rhyme and rhythm.
2
I will be able to name two ways Austen’s voice in verse differs from her voice in prose.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook, a quiet spot where you can read out loud, and your copy of Emma for one short prose comparison.
📖 PART 1 · READ IT ALOUD
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before you read: what makes a poem easy to read aloud? (No wrong answer.)

Read the opening of Austen’s comic verse out loud, twice. The first time, just enjoy the rhyme. The second time, tap the beat with your finger and feel the rhythm.

📝 JANE AUSTEN, COMIC VERSE (PUBLIC DOMAIN)
Oh! Mr. Best, you’re very bad,
And all the world shall know it;
Your base behaviour shall be sung
By me, a tuneful poet.

You used to go to Harrogate
Each summer as it came,
And why I pray should you refuse
To go this year the same?
From Austen’s light verse teasing a friend who would not make the journey to Harrogate.
⚡ PART 2 · VERSE OR PROSE VOICE?

Each description fits either the comic verse or Austen’s prose in Emma. Choose which one.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH VOICE?
Does this describe the verse or the prose?
1. Lines end in matching sounds, line after line.
2. Long, balanced sentences study a character without seeming to judge.
3. The wit is loud and out in the open, teasing someone by name.
4. The pace is even and unhurried, letting a scene unfold.
▶  In your notebook, name two ways Austen’s verse voice differs from her prose voice. Then click to compare your answer.
Two clear differences: the verse rhymes and keeps a bouncy beat, while the prose flows without rhyme; and the verse teases openly and directly, while the prose hides its wit inside long, calm sentences. Your wording may differ, as long as you name a difference in sound and a difference in how the humor works.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

A writer’s voice is not one fixed thing; it bends to fit the form. Austen can bounce in verse and glide in prose, and the same sharp wit runs under both. Memory work: drill all three passages so you can recite each one cleanly.
