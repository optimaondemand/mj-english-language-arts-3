Why One Word and Not Another

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Two words can point at the same thing and still feel completely different.
Every word carries a dictionary meaning, its denotation, and a cloud of feeling and association around it, its connotation. Slim and scrawny denote nearly the same thing, but one flatters and one does not. In the Malacandran chapters, the hrossa do not have a word that matches our word evil. When they speak of a being who has gone wrong, the translation Lewis gives is “bent” (C. S. Lewis, Out of the Silent Planet). Today you will think about why an author might prefer a quiet, physical word like that to a loud, familiar word like evil, and what the choice tells us about how the hrossa see the world.

⚡ PART 2 · WEIGH THE WORD

Here is the worked example. The word bent denotes something physically curved, pulled out of its proper straight shape. So when a hross calls a wrongdoer bent, the picture is of a creature twisted away from what it was made to be, rather than simply labeled evil. The word is quiet, concrete, and a little sad. Evil, by contrast, is loud and abstract; it sorts beings into a box. Now test your ear for connotation below.

📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

This week’s word comes from how the hrossa describe a being gone wrong. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
bent
Bent literally means curved or pulled out of a straight, proper shape. Used of a person or creature, it pictures one twisted away from what it was meant to be. It is quieter and more sorrowful than “evil”: it suggests damage rather than a simple label.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Bent is a native Old English word with no classical root behind it, a reminder that English is not only Latin and Greek dressed up. It comes simply from the old verb “to bend,” turned into an adjective for something pulled out of its proper shape.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence using bent in its connotative sense. Then rewrite it twice: once swapping in a harsher word and noticing how the feeling changes, once expanding it (“bent in a way that…”).
