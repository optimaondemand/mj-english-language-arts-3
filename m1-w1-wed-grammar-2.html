Nouns and the Words That Replace Them
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A noun names something. There are two simple questions that tell you what kind.
On Monday you found the subject of a sentence, and the subject is almost always a noun: a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. Two questions sort any noun. First: is it a general name (a common noun, like town) or a particular name (a proper noun, like Highbury, which gets a capital letter)? Second: can you see or touch it (a concrete noun, like home) or is it only an idea (an abstract noun, like happiness)? When you would rather not repeat a noun, you swap in a pronoun to stand in its place.
QUESTION 1 · HOW SPECIFIC?
Common = a general name (governess, town)
Proper = a particular name, capitalized (Miss Taylor, Highbury)
QUESTION 2 · CAN YOU SENSE IT?
Concrete = you can see/touch it (home, carriage)
Abstract = an idea or feeling (happiness, existence)
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell whether a noun is common or proper, concrete or abstract.
2
I will be able to choose the right subject pronoun to replace a noun.

Take three nouns from Austen’s opening sentence and ask the two questions about each:

Woodhouse → a particular family name, capitalized. Proper noun.
home → a general name for a thing you can touch. Common and concrete.
existence → a general name for an idea you cannot touch. Common and abstract.

Pronouns stand in for nouns

If you write “Emma is clever, and Emma loves to plan,” the second Emma feels clumsy. A pronoun fixes that: “Emma is clever, and she loves to plan.” The pronouns that can act as the subject of a sentence are a small set worth knowing by heart:

I · you · he · she · it · we · they
