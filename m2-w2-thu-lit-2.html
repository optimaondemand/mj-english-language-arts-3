📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The fastest way to learn a writer’s skill is to rebuild one of their sentences with your own materials.
When you copy a writer’s words you learn their punctuation; when you rebuild a passage in your own words you learn their thinking. Lewis describes the strange world through Ransom’s senses, naming what he sees, hears, and feels, and letting the feeling rise out of the details rather than announcing it. Today you will study how one of his descriptions is built, then write a fresh passage of your own that keeps the same sensory logic (which senses, in what order) and the same emotional logic (where the feeling turns). You are not copying his words. You are learning the moves underneath them so they become yours.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to name the sensory and emotional moves in a descriptive passage.
2
I will be able to write a fresh passage that keeps those moves with my own content.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet, Chapters 5 to 8, your writing notebook, and a quiet spot to read aloud.
📚 PART 1 · STUDY THE MOVES
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When a description grabs you, what usually does it: the big idea, or the small exact details? (No wrong answer.)

Open your own copy to a short descriptive passage in Chapters 5 to 8, one where Ransom takes in the strangeness around him. Read it slowly and ask three questions. Below is a worked example using an original passage written for this lesson, not from the book, so you can see the moves clearly:

“At first there was only the hum in his ears. Then the smell of warm metal, sharp and faintly sweet. He opened his eyes to a light too gold to be the sun, and against his will, his fear loosened into astonishment.”
The moves:
1. Sensory order: sound first, then smell, then sight. The senses arrive one at a time.
2. Concrete detail: “warm metal, sharp and faintly sweet,” not just “a strange smell.”
3. Emotional turn at the end: fear gives way to astonishment in the last clause, and the feeling is shown, not announced.
⚡ PART 2 · HOW STRONG IS THE IMITATION?

A student tried to imitate the moves above. For each version, decide how well it keeps the sensory and emotional logic.

⚡ TRY IT · RATE EACH IMITATION
Place each attempt: weak, partway, or strong imitation of the moves.
1. “First came the cold against her cheek. Then the smell of wet stone. She looked up to a sky the wrong shade of green, and her panic eased into wonder.”
2. “She was scared at first but then she felt amazed. It was all very strange and new to her.”
3. “The smell of crushed leaves filled the cabin, green and bitter. She breathed it in for a long time.”
✏️ PART 3 · WRITE, THEN READ ALOUD

Now do it from the book itself. Choose a short descriptive passage from your own copy of Chapters 5 to 8 and rebuild it with your own content, keeping the same sensory order and emotional turn.

▶  In your notebook, write your imitation. Then read both the original and your version aloud, one after the other. Click for what to listen for.
Reading aloud is the test. Listen for three things: Do your senses arrive in the same order as his? Are your details concrete, not vague? Does your feeling turn where his turns? If a line sounds flat next to Lewis, look for the abstract word and replace it with something you could see, hear, or smell. Keep this passage; it becomes Step 2 of your story ladder in this week’s Quick Write.
➡️ WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

You learn a master’s craft by rebuilding it with your own materials. The moves you copied today (senses in order, concrete detail, a feeling that turns) are now tools you own. Memory work: keep building Drill Passage 1, a little each day.
