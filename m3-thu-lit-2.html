Lesson 3.4
The Drumbeat of “When,” and the Pivot
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Repeating the same opening builds pressure, and then one new word releases it.
Look at the first four lines of the poem. Each one begins with the word When, and three of them begin with When I. Repeating an opening word or phrase at the start of several lines is called anaphora, and building those lines in the same shape is parallelism. Together they create a drumbeat: When… When… When… When…, piling up the proofs and figures until they feel like too much. Then line five opens differently: How soon unaccountable I became tired and sick. That change of opening word is the pivot. The drumbeat stops, the speaker turns from the lecture to his own feeling, and within three more lines wonder has overtaken calculation. The repetition is not decoration; it is what makes the turn land.

WHEN I HEARD THE LEARN’D ASTRONOMER · WALT WHITMAN (1865)
When I heard the learn’d astronomer,
When the proofs, the figures, were ranged in columns before me,
When I was shown the charts and diagrams, to add, divide, and measure them,
When I sitting heard the astronomer where he lectured with much applause in the lecture-room,
How soon unaccountable I became tired and sick,
Till rising and gliding out I wander’d off by myself,
In the mystical moist night-air, and from time to time,
Look’d up in perfect silence at the stars.
This poem is in the public domain.
Lines 1 to 4 open the same way → the repeated When is the anaphora; the matching shapes are the parallelism.
Line 5 opens with “How soon” → the pivot: the drumbeat stops and the poem turns inward, then outdoors.

📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

At the pivot, Whitman says he grew “unaccountable” tired and sick. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
unaccountable
Unaccountable means impossible to explain or account for. The speaker cannot give a reason for his weariness, which is the point: feeling and wonder are not the kind of thing the lecture’s proofs can measure.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Unaccountable is built on account, which reached English through French but goes back to Latin computare, “to count or reckon.” The same root gives us computer and recount, both about tallying something up.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence using unaccountable. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“unaccountable because…”).
