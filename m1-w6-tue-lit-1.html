Lesson 1.27
Reading One Paragraph Slowly
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A single paragraph can tell you everything about a person, if you slow down enough to hear it.
Mr. Elton has married, and his new wife has arrived in Highbury. Mrs. Elton talks a great deal, and almost all of it circles back to herself: her brother’s fine carriage, her sister’s grand estate, her own taste and importance. Austen does not step in to say “Mrs. Elton was vain.” She lets Mrs. Elton say it for herself, one boast at a time. Today you will take one paragraph and read it the slow way: not for what happens, but for what each sentence quietly reveals. When you read this slowly, you are asking a single question of every line: what is Austen doing here?
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what one line of a paragraph reveals about Mrs. Elton.
2
I will be able to describe how Austen builds a character through speech rather than a label.

📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN SLOW DOWN

Read Volume II, Chapters 13 to 18, where Mrs. Elton enters the story. Then return here and look closely at one short paragraph of her talk.

📖 YOUR READING
Emma: full text (PDF), Vol. II, Ch. 13–18  [Teacher: confirm the Canvas file link on deploy.]
THE PARAGRAPH WE WILL READ CLOSELY
“I always say a woman cannot have too many resources; and I feel very thankful that I have so many myself as to be quite independent of society. Mr. E. is so fond of music that I am sure he would never have made up to me if I had not been able to sing. So, as I always wished, I have given up the practice of it a little. But I assure you it costs me nothing.”
From Mrs. Elton's conversation in Vol. II
⚡ PART 2 · LINE BY LINE: WHAT IS AUSTEN DOING?

For each line, choose what it quietly reveals about Mrs. Elton.

⚡ TRY IT · READ THE QUIET MEANING
What is Austen showing us in each line?
1. “I feel very thankful that I have so many myself.”
2. “quite independent of society.”
3. “he would never have made up to me if I had not been able to sing.”
4. “I assure you it costs me nothing.”
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Mrs. Elton is forever managing other people’s affairs uninvited. Austen calls such a person “officious.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
officious
Officious means too eager to offer help or advice that no one asked for, in a way that feels like meddling. It is not the same as “official.” Mrs. Elton is officious: she takes charge of Jane Fairfax’s future without ever being asked.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
You met this word’s root back in Week 4. See if you can recall it before reading on: officious traces to Latin officium, “duty.” If it does not come back to you, revisit that week’s Word Roots note.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing Mrs. Elton using officious. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“officious in the way she…”).

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

When a writer never tells you outright that a character is vain, the proof lives in the lines themselves. Slow reading is how you find it. Memory work: keep learning passage 3, “Seldom, very seldom, does complete truth belong to any human disclosure.”
