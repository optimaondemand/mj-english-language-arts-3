Lesson 1.19
Writing in Another’s Voice
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
To write as a character, you first have to hear how he sounds.
One of the best ways to understand a character is to try writing as that character. Today you will write a few sentences in Frank Churchill’s voice. Frank is charming, quick, and a little too pleased with himself. He flatters; he turns a compliment with ease; he keeps things light even when something serious is underneath. Before you imitate him, you will study how he sounds, then practice matching that sound. Copying a voice on purpose teaches you to notice the small choices, the flattering phrase, the cheerful dodge, that make a character that character.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe the features of Frank Churchill’s way of speaking.
2
I will be able to write a short passage that matches his voice.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your copy of Emma (or the linked PDF), Vol. II, Ch. 1–6, and your writing notebook.
📚 PART 1 · HEAR THE VOICE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of someone you know who is very charming. How do you know? (No wrong answer.)

Reread how Frank speaks when he first arrives. Here is a short line in his manner, with the features marked so you can hear them:

“What a delightful spot Highbury is! I have heard so much of it, and now I find the reality even more charming than the report.”
Flattery of the place. Exclamation for energy. Easy, pleasing phrasing that asks nothing hard of anyone.
⚡ PART 2 · WHICH LINE SOUNDS LIKE FRANK?

For each pair, choose the line that matches Frank’s charming, light voice.

✏️ PART 3 · WRITE IT YOURSELF

In your notebook, write 3 to 4 sentences in Frank’s voice. Imagine he is praising a dinner, a song, or the Highbury weather. Use at least one exclamation and one compliment, and keep the tone light.

▶  Write your passage first. Then click to see a model and a checklist.
Model: “What a charming evening this has been! I declare I have never heard a song so prettily sung, nor met company so kind to a newcomer. Highbury has quite won me over.”   Checklist: Did you flatter someone or something? Use an exclamation? Keep the tone light and pleasing? If yes, you have caught his voice.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

When you write in Frank’s voice and it comes out charming and a little evasive, you have learned something about him that no summary could teach. Hold onto that as the novel goes on: the same charm that pleases Highbury may also be hiding something. Memory work: drill all your memorized passages this week.
