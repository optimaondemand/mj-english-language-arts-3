Lesson 3.3
Marks That Make a Reader Pause
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Punctuation tells a reader when to pause, and that pause carries meaning.
A poet controls the speed of a poem with small marks. Three are worth knowing. The line break is the most basic: the end of a line asks for a small pause, even when there is no comma, so the reader breathes and the next line lands fresh. The dash is a longer, sharper pause that can set a thought apart or signal a sudden shift. The ellipsis, the three dots, is a trailing-off pause that leaves a thought open, as if the voice fades into silence. Whitman lets his lines and commas slow the poem down at the end, so that the last line feels like a held breath under the stars. When you write your own poem, these marks let you direct the reader the way a conductor sets a tempo.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to name what a line break, a dash, and an ellipsis do to the pace of a poem.
2
I will be able to choose a mark to create the pause I want.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you read these dots out loud, “and then, slowly …” what does your voice do? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the same short sentence punctuated three ways. Read each one aloud and notice how the pause changes:

Line break: “I rose
    and walked outside.” → a small breath at the break. A gentle pause.
Dash: “I rose — and walked outside.” → a sharper, sudden pause. A break in thought.
Ellipsis: “I rose … and walked outside.” → the voice trails and waits. An open, fading pause.
The dash above is shown so you can see the mark itself; that is the punctuation this lesson teaches.
⚡ PART 2 · HOW LONG IS THE PAUSE?

Place each mark by the kind of pause it creates, from shortest to longest.

⚡ TRY IT · SHORTEST TO LONGEST
Choose where each mark belongs on the scale of pause length.
1. The line break creates a pause that is…
2. The dash creates a pause that is…
3. The ellipsis creates a pause that is…
⚡ PART 3 · WHICH MARK FITS?

Choose the mark that best creates the effect described.

⚡ TRY IT · PICK THE MARK
Choose the best answer.
1. You want the last line of your poem to fade into silence. Best mark?
2. You want a sudden turn to feel abrupt and set apart. Best mark?
3. You want a gentle breath between two images, with no heavy stop. Best mark?
▶  In your notebook, write one short line and punctuate its ending three ways: a plain line break, a dash, and an ellipsis. Click to see an example.
Plain: “The room went quiet” (line break)   Dash: “The room went quiet —”   Ellipsis: “The room went quiet …” Read all three aloud and notice how the pause grows.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Marks set the tempo: the line break breathes, the dash breaks, the ellipsis fades. Tomorrow you will look at how Whitman repeats “When I” and then pivots at “How soon.” Memory work: keep building the poem; aim to hold the first six lines.
