Lesson 1.21
Words That Join
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Some joining words treat two ideas as equals. Others bend one idea under another.
A conjunction is a word that joins. There are two kinds you should be able to tell apart. A coordinating conjunction joins two things of equal weight: two words, two phrases, or two complete ideas. The common ones are and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet. In “Emma was clever, but she was often wrong,” the word but joins two ideas that could each stand alone. A subordinating conjunction works differently: it makes one idea depend on another, so that the idea it begins can no longer stand by itself. Words like because, although, when, since, if, and while do this. In “Emma apologized because she had been unkind,” the word because turns “she had been unkind” into a reason that leans on the rest of the sentence. Learning to tell the two apart helps you see how a writer ranks ideas: equal, or one beneath the other.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find the conjunction in a sentence.
2
I will be able to tell a coordinating conjunction from a subordinating one.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which joining word treats the two ideas as equals, and which makes one lean on the other? (No wrong answer. Just notice.)

Here is the test. Take the words that follow the joining word and read them alone. If they form a complete sentence on their own, the conjunction is most likely coordinating. If they cannot stand alone, it is subordinating.

“Emma meant well, BUT she misjudged Harriet.” → “she misjudged Harriet” stands alone. Coordinating.
“Harriet trusted Emma BECAUSE Emma seemed so sure.” → “Emma seemed so sure” here leans on the first part. Subordinating.
“Mr. Knightley was honest ALTHOUGH it was hard.” → “it was hard” depends on the rest. Subordinating.

▶  In your notebook, write one sentence about a character using a coordinating conjunction and one using a subordinating conjunction. Then click to see examples.
Coordinating: “Frank charmed everyone, and no one suspected him.”   Subordinating: “Because Frank kept a secret, his kindness was partly a performance.” (Your own will differ; just check that the subordinating one begins a thought that cannot stand alone.)
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Coordinating conjunctions set ideas side by side; subordinating ones rank one beneath another. As you read this week, notice how often Austen uses a quiet “but” or “although” to undercut a character’s grand claim. Memory work: drill every passage you have memorized this module; say each one aloud from memory.
