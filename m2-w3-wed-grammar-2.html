Verbs That Describe

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A verb form can stop acting like a verb and start describing a noun.
A participle is a form of a verb used as an adjective. The two kinds you will meet most often are the present participle, ending in -ing (singing, gliding, rising), and the past participle, often ending in -ed or -en (frightened, broken, hidden). A participial phrase is the participle plus the words that go with it, and the whole phrase describes a noun: Gliding across the water, the boat made no sound. The phrase “gliding across the water” tells you about the boat. Lewis uses these constantly to keep his alien world in motion. Watch the comma: when a participial phrase opens a sentence, it is usually followed by one.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to recognize a participial phrase in a sentence.
2
I will be able to name the noun a participial phrase describes.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
In “Startled by the sound, Ransom froze,” which word is the describing part doing the work? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the test. Find a verb form ending in -ing, -ed, or -en that is not the main action of the sentence, then ask which noun it describes. If it describes a noun, it is a participle, and the words traveling with it form a participial phrase.

“Singing in their deep voices, the hrossa rowed.” → describes the hrossa. Participial phrase: singing in their deep voices.
“Hidden among the stalks, Ransom watched.” → describes Ransom. Participial phrase: hidden among the stalks.
“The sky, glowing a pale rose, darkened slowly.” → describes the sky. Participial phrase: glowing a pale rose.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

For each sentence, choose the noun that the italic participial phrase describes.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES THE PHRASE DESCRIBE?
Which noun does the participial phrase describe?
1. Frightened by the noise, the creature darted away.
2. The moon, rising over the hills, lit the valley.
3. Ransom studied the tower, carved from black stone.
4. Speaking slowly, the hross taught Ransom new words.
▶  In your notebook, write two sentences about a creature in an imagined world, each opening with a participial phrase. Then click to see examples.
Examples: “Drifting on the warm current, the swimmer studied the shore.”   “Wrapped in pale mist, the city slept.” (Your own sentences will differ; check that the opening phrase describes the noun right after the comma.)
