Lesson 1.24
Reading Between the Lines
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A character can tell you what she is like without ever meaning to.
To infer is to figure out something the text does not say outright, using clues it does give. Austen almost never tells us “Mrs. Elton is a snob.” Instead she lets Mrs. Elton talk, and from the talk we draw the conclusion ourselves. At the tea table Mrs. Elton mentions her sister’s estate, her own “resources,” and how she means to improve poor Highbury, all in the space of a few breaths. Austen states none of the judgment. She gives us the speech and trusts us to add it up. Today you will read the clues and practice the move every strong reader makes: from what is said, to what it shows. Always keep your inference tied to an actual line, so it is reading and not guessing.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to make an inference about a character from what she says.
2
I will be able to tie each inference to the line that supports it.

📖 YOUR READING
Emma: full text (PDF), Vol. II, Ch. 7–12  [Teacher: confirm the Canvas file link on deploy.]
⚡ PART 2 · FROM THE LINE TO THE INFERENCE

For each thing Mrs. Elton says, choose the most reasonable inference. Stay close to the words.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES THE LINE TELL US?
What can you fairly infer?
1. Mrs. Elton mentions her sister’s estate, “Maple Grove,” in nearly every conversation.
2. She announces she will “bring a little more society” to a place she has just arrived in.
3. After barely meeting him, she refers to Mr. Knightley simply as “Knightley.”
4. She insists on managing Jane Fairfax’s affairs while praising her own “taste” for arranging things.
▶  In your notebook, write one inference about Mrs. Elton and the exact line that supports it. Then click to see a model.
Model: Inference: Mrs. Elton values money over manners. Support: she measures Highbury and its people by “resources” and by Maple Grove rather than by their kindness. Your own inference will differ; just be sure a real line backs it.
