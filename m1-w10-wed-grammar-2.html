Lesson 1.48
Editing Your Own Draft
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Editing is reading your own writing as if a stranger wrote it.
You have a draft. Today you clean it. Three errors are common and easy to fix once you can spot them. A run-on joins two complete sentences with only a comma or with nothing at all; the fix is a period, a semicolon, or a joining word like and or but. A fragment is a piece pretending to be a sentence but missing a subject or a verb; the fix is to complete the thought. A subject-verb mismatch happens when a singular subject takes a plural verb, or the reverse: “Emma walk” should be “Emma walks.” Read your draft slowly, one sentence at a time, and these errors show themselves.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to spot a run-on, a fragment, and a subject-verb mismatch.
2
I will be able to fix each of those errors in my own draft.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your essay draft open in Word and about 10 minutes for an editing pass.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you reread your own writing, which is harder for you to catch? (No wrong answer.)

Watch one error get fixed. The bolding here shows the change; in the practice below, nothing will be marked for you.

Run-on: “Emma means well, she still causes harm.” → Fixed: “Emma means well, but she still causes harm.”
Fragment: “Because she trusts her own judgment.” → Fixed: “She acts because she trusts her own judgment.”
Mismatch: “The characters in Highbury watches each other.” → Fixed: “The characters in Highbury watch each other.”
▶  Read this sentence and decide how to fix it before you click: “Mr. Knightley speaks plainly he tells Emma the truth.”
This is a run-on: two complete sentences with nothing between them. Fix it with a period (“…plainly. He tells…”), a semicolon (“…plainly; he tells…”), or a joining word (“…plainly, and he tells…”). Any of the three is correct.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Read each sentence and name the error it contains.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE ERROR
What is wrong with each sentence?
1. “Emma is clever, she does not always understand people.”
2. “Although Emma tries to help her friend Harriet.”
3. “Emma learn an important lesson at Box Hill.”
4. “By the end of the novel, Emma sees her own mistakes clearly.”
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Make one editing pass through your whole draft today, hunting only for these three errors. Reading aloud is the best test. Memory work: your recitation is graded this week. Practice your passage standing up, at the pace you will use to record it.
