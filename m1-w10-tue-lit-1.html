Building the Essay You Have Been Preparing All Module
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A strong argument does not hide from the other side. It answers it.
You have read all of Emma, tracked your evidence, and practiced naming objections all module. Today you turn that into a draft. A good argumentative essay has three jobs. First, it states a clear position and answers the central question. Second, it proves that position with specific evidence from the text. Third, and this is the part that separates a fine essay from an excellent one, it states the strongest objection a thoughtful reader could raise, takes it seriously, and answers it. Your essay must do this for at least two objections. Today you will draft; you will also have a chance to confer with your teacher about your plan before you finish.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to turn my position into a clear thesis backed by specific evidence.
2
I will be able to state a strong objection to my view and answer it.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your Reading Journal and Organizer (your evidence bank), your Character Collage notes, and a Word document started for the essay.
📖 PART 1 · FIND YOUR POSITION
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
After the whole novel, which feels closer to your honest answer about Emma? (No wrong answer.)

Your thesis is your answer to the central question, stated in one sentence you could defend. Then gather your three or four strongest pieces of evidence from your Organizer. A short, exact quotation beats a long, vague one.

💬 CONFERENCING
Before you write the body, bring your one-sentence thesis and your two planned objections to your teacher conference. [Teacher: schedule a short conference; confirm the sign-up link on deploy.]
⚡ PART 2 · WHICH MOVE IS IT?

Each sentence does one job in an argument. Choose the job it does.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE MOVE
What is each sentence doing in the essay?
1. “A reader might fairly say Emma never truly changes; yet her apology to Miss Bates marks a real turn.”
2. “Emma comes to know herself only after her own confidence has hurt the people she meant to help.”
3. “Mr. Knightley’s ‘It was badly done, indeed!’ lands because Emma knows he is right.”
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

A useful word for your essay is “concede.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
concede
Concede means to admit that part of the other side is true. In an argument you concede a point to your opponent before you answer it, which makes your answer stronger, not weaker.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Concede comes from Latin con- (“together”) and cedere (“to yield, to go”). The same root cedere shows up in proceed (to go forward) and recede (to go back).
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence about your essay using concede. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“I concede that… and yet…”). Use the best version to start a counterargument paragraph.
