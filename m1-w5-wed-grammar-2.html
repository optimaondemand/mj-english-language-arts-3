The Last Part, and the Whole Set

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
One small word carries pure feeling. With it, you have met all eight.
The last part of speech is the interjection: a word or short burst that shows sudden feeling and stands mostly on its own, often with an exclamation mark. Oh! Alas! Well, Indeed! An interjection is not woven into the grammar of the sentence; it sits beside it, carrying surprise, dismay, or delight. Now you have met all eight parts of speech. Every word in every sentence does one of these eight jobs: a noun names; a pronoun stands in for a noun; a verb shows action or links; an adjective describes a noun; an adverb describes a verb, adjective, or other adverb; a preposition shows position or relationship; a conjunction joins; and an interjection exclaims. The same word can do different jobs in different sentences, so always ask what the word is doing, not just what it looks like.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to spot an interjection and explain what feeling it carries.
2
I will be able to name the job a given word does among the eight parts of speech.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you stubbed your toe, which sound would come out first? (No wrong answer.)

An interjection can usually be lifted out without breaking the rest of the sentence. Here is the whole set in one place, with one Austen example each.

Noun: Emma  ·  Pronoun: she  ·  Verb: walked  ·  Adjective: clever
Adverb: quickly  ·  Preposition: through (the garden)  ·  Conjunction: but  ·  Interjection: Indeed!
One sentence using several: “Oh! Emma walked quickly through the garden, but she said nothing.”
▶  In your notebook, name the part of speech for each word in “Alas, the foolish plan failed quickly.” Then click to check.
Alas = interjection · the = article (a kind of adjective) · foolish = adjective · plan = noun · failed = verb · quickly = adverb. Six different jobs in one short sentence.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Name the job the italicized word is doing in each sentence.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH PART OF SPEECH?
Choose the job of the word in italics.
1. Well! Mrs. Elton had quite an opinion of herself.
2. She admired the elegant carriage.
3. Emma was polite, although she disliked the visit.
4. She spoke proudly of her connections.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

You now know all eight parts of speech. The same word can change jobs from sentence to sentence, so always ask what it is doing. Memory work: drill every passage you have memorized this module; aim to say each one aloud, smoothly, from memory.
