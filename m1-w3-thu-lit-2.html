Page Against Screen
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A film can show you a face the page only describes.
The same scene can be told two ways. On the page, Austen controls what you know by staying close to Emma’s thoughts: you misread Mr. Elton because Emma does. A film cannot get inside Emma’s head the way prose can, so it uses other tools, the actors’ faces, the music, a close-up, a pause, to suggest what she is thinking. When you compare a book scene to its film version, you are not asking which is better. You are asking what each medium can do that the other cannot, and what is gained or lost when the proposal moves from page to screen.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to name one thing the prose does that a film cannot, and one thing a film does that prose cannot.
2
I will be able to say what is gained or lost when the proposal scene moves to the screen.

ON THE PAGE
Austen keeps us inside Emma’s mind: she is “in a state of utmost amazement,” and we feel her shock as it arrives. The narrator can report a private thought no camera could film. That closeness is the prose’s advantage.
⚡ PART 2 · PAGE OR SCREEN?

For each strength, choose which medium does it better.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT EACH MEDIUM DOES BEST
Which medium handles each thing better?
1. Showing exactly what Emma is thinking the instant Mr. Elton speaks.
2. Letting you see Emma’s expression change in real time.
3. Building tension with music before anyone speaks.
4. Keeping the narrator’s dry, ironic comments about Mr. Elton.
📝 PART 3 · NAME THE TRADE-OFF

Decide your own answer, write it in your notebook, then check one model.

▶  In two sentences, name one thing the film gains and one thing it loses in the proposal scene. Then click to compare with a model answer.
Model: The film gains the actors’ faces, so we watch Emma’s shock arrive instead of being told about it. It loses Austen’s ironic narrator, who on the page lets us laugh at Mr. Elton even while Emma is mortified. Your answer should name one gain and one loss in your own words.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Page and screen tell the same story with different tools. The page owns the inner life; the screen owns the face and the music. Hold on to the gain-and-loss note you wrote; it sharpens how you read. Memory work: drill the opening sentence, and keep working on “It was badly done, indeed!”
