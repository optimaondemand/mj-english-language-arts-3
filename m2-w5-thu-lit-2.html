Lesson 2.24
Build Your Scene, Then Revise the Words
Literature Lesson · VR as a drafting tool
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When you can walk through your scene, you find out what your words left out.
Lewis built the world of Malacandra so completely that the planet feels real: the colors, the height of the plants, the way sound carries over water. Today you do something similar in reverse. You have been drafting a first-contact scene for your own story. You will build that scene in the class VR space, then walk back to your draft and ask a hard question: does my writing match what I built? Often the build reveals gaps. You pictured a tall creature but never told the reader its height; you imagined cold light but wrote only “it was bright.” The VR is not a performance to be graded. It is a tool that exposes the difference between what you imagined and what you actually put on the page, so you can fix the words.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to build one key scene from my story in the VR space.
2
I will be able to compare the build to my draft and find places where the words fall short.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your current story draft, your VR headset, and your writing notebook. Choose any Location you like to build in.
🌍 PART 1 · PICK THE SCENE TO BUILD
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which moment in your story would gain the most from being built and walked through? (No wrong answer.)
🧮 YOUR VR BUILD
Enter the class VR space and choose any Location to work in. Build the one scene you picked: place the ground, the sky or light, the main object or landform, and a simple stand-in for the other being. You are sketching in three dimensions, not making it perfect. [Teacher: VR platform/scene TBD. Mark Tier-1 completion when the student has built the scene.]
⚡ PART 2 · WHERE DO THE WORDS FALL SHORT?

Stand in your built scene and look around. For each gap below, decide what kind of revision it calls for.

⚡ TRY IT · DIAGNOSE THE GAP
What does each gap tell you to do in your draft?
1. In VR the creature towers over you, but your draft never says how tall it is.
2. Your draft says the light was “bright,” but in VR it is a cold, pale blue.
3. Your draft mentions a forest, but the scene you built is bare rock and sky.
▶  In your notebook, list two things you can see in your VR build that your draft does not yet tell the reader. Then click for an example of turning one into a sentence.
Gap noticed: the ground is spongy and dark red. Revised sentence: “The ground gave slightly under each step, dark red and damp, like walking on cooled lava.” You can use an adjective clause here too: “the ground, which gave under each step, was dark red.”
➡️ WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT
1
Week 5 Quick Write (story ladder, step 5)
Write up your VR comparison and revise two or three passages of your story for precision. (Typed Word document. See the assignment page.)
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Building a scene shows you the gap between what you imagined and what you actually wrote. The goal is not a finished VR room; it is sharper sentences. Memory work: keep learning passage 2, the Oyarsa’s judgment on the bent race, and say it aloud once more before you log off.
