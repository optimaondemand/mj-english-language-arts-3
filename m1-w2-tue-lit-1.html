Lesson 1.7
What People Reveal When They Speak
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A character’s own words tell you more than the narrator ever could.
Austen lets us judge people by how they talk. Harriet Smith, the pretty, agreeable girl Emma takes up as a friend, speaks with constant uncertainty, forever asking what others think before she dares an opinion of her own. Mr. Knightley speaks the opposite way: plainly, and willing to tell Emma when she is wrong. As you read Chapters 4 to 9, listen to the voices. What does a person’s way of speaking show about who they are?
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what a character’s words reveal about who they are.
2
I will be able to contrast how Harriet and Mr. Knightley speak.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your copy of Emma (or the linked PDF), Vol. I, Ch. 4–9, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN LISTEN
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of two people you know who speak very differently. What does each one’s way of talking tell you about them? (No wrong answer.)

Read Volume I, Chapters 4 to 9. Pay special attention to how Harriet answers a question and how Mr. Knightley states an opinion.

Emma: full text (PDF), Vol. I, Ch. 4–9  [Teacher: confirm the Canvas file link on deploy.]
⚡ PART 2 · WHAT DOES THE LINE REVEAL?

For each line, choose what it shows about the speaker.

⚡ TRY IT · LISTEN FOR CHARACTER
What does each line reveal?
1. Harriet: “I do not know, whatever you think is best.”
2. Mr. Knightley, to Emma: “You have been no friend to Harriet Smith.”
3. Emma: “I lay it down as a general rule that a woman is to marry as she ought.”
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Austen calls Harriet “artless.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
artless
Artless means without cunning or guile: simple, sincere, and easily read. It is part compliment, part warning: Harriet is honest, but also easy to influence.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Artless is built from art, ultimately Latin ars/artis (“skill, craft”), plus the native English ending -less: literally “without craft.” The same root shows up in artisan and artifact.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing Harriet using artless. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“artless in a way that…”).
