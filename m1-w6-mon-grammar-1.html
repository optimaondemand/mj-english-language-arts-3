Lesson 1.26
When a Verb Hands Something Over
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Some verbs give one thing to one person. The sentence holds both.
You already know that a verb can take a direct object: the thing the verb acts on. In “Emma gave a gift,” the gift is what was given. But many sentences answer a second question: given to whom? In “Emma gave Harriet a gift,” the gift is still the direct object, and Harriet is the person who received it. That receiver is the indirect object. It sits right after the verb and right before the direct object, and it never has the word to or for in front of it. Verbs of giving and telling pull this pattern in: give, send, tell, offer, show, hand, teach, write. Learning to spot the quiet receiver helps you read a sentence the way it is actually built.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find the direct object and the indirect object in a giving or telling sentence.
2
I will be able to explain why the receiver comes before the thing received.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
In “Mr. Knightley sent Emma a note,” who or what ends up holding the note? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the test for the receiver: after the verb, ask to whom? or for whom? The answer, if it has no to or for sitting in front of it, is the indirect object.

“Emma gave Harriet a portrait.” → Gave what? a portrait (direct object). Gave it to whom? Harriet is the indirect object.
“Mr. Knightley told Emma the truth.” → Told what? the truth. Told it to whom? Emma is the indirect object.
“Mrs. Weston wrote her a long letter.” → Wrote what? a letter. Wrote it to whom? her is the indirect object.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

In each sentence, which word is the receiver (the indirect object)?

⚡ TRY IT · FIND THE RECEIVER
Pick the indirect object: the person the verb hands something to.
1. Emma offered Mrs. Elton a seat.
2. Mr. Weston showed the visitors his gardens.
3. Frank told us a long story.
4. Miss Bates handed Jane a letter.
▶  In your notebook, write one giving sentence and one telling sentence, each with a receiver and a thing received. Then click to see examples.
Giving: “Emma gave Harriet some advice.” (receiver: Harriet; thing received: advice)   Telling: “Mr. Knightley told Emma a hard truth.” (receiver: Emma; thing received: a hard truth). Check that your receiver has no to or for in front of it.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

When a verb gives or tells, look for two things after it: the receiver first, then the thing received. On Wednesday you will hunt for these receivers in real sentences from the novel. Memory work: begin learning passage 3, “Seldom, very seldom, does complete truth belong to any human disclosure.”
