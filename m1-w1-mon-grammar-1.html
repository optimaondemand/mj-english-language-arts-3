Lesson 1.1
Finding the Parts of a Sentence
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Every sentence is built on two words: a subject and a verb.
The subject is the person or thing the sentence is about. The verb is what the subject does or is. Find those two and you have the backbone of the sentence; every other word just adds detail. Here is a sentence with each word labeled:

↑ the SUBJECT and the VERB are the backbone ↑
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find the subject and verb in a sentence.
2
I will be able to tell whether a word is a subject, verb, adjective, adverb, or article.
3
I will be able to find the subject and verb hidden inside one of Austen’s long sentences.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil
▶
About 5–10 minutes of short, focused practice
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you read a sentence, which part do you usually notice first? (No wrong answer.)

Watch how each word gets named. Read the sentence, then read the questions beside it. Each question points to one word:

“The young lady walked gracefully.”
What is happening? → walked. That’s the verb.
Who walked? → lady. That’s the subject.
Walked how? → gracefully. That’s an adverb.
What kind of lady? → young. That’s an adjective.
And “the”? That’s an article, pointing to the noun.
🔑 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
1
Find the VERB. Ask: what is happening?
2
Ask “who?” or “what?” in front of the verb. That answer is the SUBJECT.
SUBJECT + VERB = the backbone. Every other word just adds detail.

📚 PART 3 · A LONGER SENTENCE

Here is the sentence you are learning by heart this week. It is long, but it still has just one subject and one verb. Read past the words that only describe Emma, and find what the sentence actually says she did:

“Emma Woodhouse, handsome, clever, and rich, with a comfortable home and happy disposition, seemed to unite some of the best blessings of existence…”
⚡ TRY IT
Which words are the verb? (The verb is what the sentence says Emma did.)
