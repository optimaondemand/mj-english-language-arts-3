One Idea, or Two Joined Together

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A sentence can carry one complete thought, or two thoughts held together as equals.
A clause is a group of words with its own subject and verb. A clause that can stand alone as a sentence is called an independent clause: it expresses a complete thought. A simple sentence is one independent clause: The ship rose into the dark. A compound sentence joins two independent clauses of equal weight into one: The ship rose into the dark, and the stars grew sharp. Each half could stand on its own, yet Lewis links them so the two events feel like one moment. As you read this week, notice how often he sets two equal observations side by side; the rhythm of his prose depends on it.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell a simple sentence from a compound sentence.
2
I will be able to find the two independent clauses inside a compound sentence.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which of these sounds like one finished thought, and which sounds like two thoughts joined? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the test. Cover the joining word and the comma. If both halves could stand alone as sentences, you have a compound sentence. If only one complete thought is left, it is simple.

“Ransom studied the strange landscape.” → one complete thought. Simple sentence.
“Ransom studied the landscape, but he could name nothing he saw.” → two complete thoughts joined. Compound sentence.
“He listened and waited.” → one subject doing two things; the second half cannot stand alone. Still simple.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Read each sentence. Is it simple or compound?

⚡ TRY IT · SIMPLE OR COMPOUND?
Choose the kind of sentence.
1. The engines fell silent, so the cabin filled with a deep stillness.
2. Ransom drifted and turned in the weightless air.
3. The window framed the planet, and its surface glowed a faint rose.
4. Wary of his captors, Ransom said nothing.
▶  In your notebook, write one simple sentence about the journey, then turn it into a compound sentence by adding a second equal thought. Then click to see an example.
Simple: “The stars looked closer than ever.”   Compound: “The stars looked closer than ever, and Ransom forgot to be afraid.” (Your sentences will differ; check that both halves of the compound one could stand alone.)
