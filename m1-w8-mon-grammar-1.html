Lesson 1.36
Making a Pronoun Match the Word It Stands For
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A pronoun is a stand-in. It has to match the word it stands for.
A pronoun like she, he, it, or they takes the place of a noun so you do not have to repeat the name. The noun it points back to is its antecedent. The rule is simple: a pronoun must match its antecedent in number (one or more than one) and in gender (he, she, it). So we write “Emma loves her father,” not “Emma loves their father,” because Emma is one person, a she. The tricky cases are words like everyone, each, and nobody: they sound like a crowd, but each one counts as a single person, so a singular pronoun matches them.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find the antecedent a pronoun points back to.
2
I will be able to choose a pronoun that matches its antecedent in number and gender.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
“Harriet packed ___ things and left.” Which word sounds right to your ear? (No wrong answer.)

To check a pronoun, point back to its antecedent and ask two questions: One or more than one? He, she, or it? Then match.

“Mr. Knightley keeps his counsel.” → antecedent is “Mr. Knightley” (one, he), so his matches.
“The Bates ladies value their friends.” → antecedent is “ladies” (more than one), so their matches.
“Everyone in Highbury had his or her opinion.” → “everyone” is singular, so a singular pronoun matches.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Choose the pronoun that matches the antecedent in each sentence.

⚡ TRY IT · MATCH THE PRONOUN
Which pronoun matches its antecedent?
1. Emma finally admitted that ___ judgment had failed.
2. The villagers gathered, and ___ talk turned to the wedding.
3. Each of the gentlemen offered ___ arm.
4. Box Hill was famous for ___ wide view.
▶  In your notebook, write one sentence about a character, then add a second sentence that uses a pronoun to refer back to that character. Then click to see an example.
Example: “Jane Fairfax kept her secret for months. She bore the strain quietly.” The antecedent is Jane Fairfax (one, she), so “her” and “she” both match. Check that your pronoun points clearly to one named person.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

A pronoun is only as clear as the noun it points back to. As you read this week, notice how Austen keeps long sentences clear by matching every pronoun to the right person. Memory work: drill all three of your passages so you can recite each one cleanly.
