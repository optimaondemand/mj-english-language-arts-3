Lesson 1.28
Finding the Quiet Receiver
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The receiver hides in plain sight. The trick is telling it apart from its disguise.
On Monday you met the receiver: the word that comes right after a giving or telling verb, with no to or for in front of it. Today watch the disguise. English lets you say the same idea two ways. “Emma gave Harriet the news” puts the receiver bare, right after the verb, so Harriet is the indirect object. But “Emma gave the news to Harriet” moves Harriet into a phrase starting with to. Once the word to or for appears in front of the receiver, it is no longer the indirect object; it is part of a phrase. Same meaning, different build. Knowing the difference keeps you from labeling a word by guesswork.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to find the indirect object in sentences drawn from the novel.
2
I will be able to tell a bare receiver from one tucked inside a “to” or “for” phrase.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Two ways to say it: “Emma gave Harriet the news” and “Emma gave the news to Harriet.” Which one puts Harriet right after the verb? (No wrong answer.)

The rule of thumb: a real indirect object has nothing in front of it and stands between the verb and the thing received. The moment you see to or for, the receiver has stepped into a phrase instead.

“Mrs. Elton offered Jane her help.” → bare receiver between verb and thing. Jane is the indirect object.
“Mrs. Elton offered her help to Jane.” → ‘to’ appears, so Jane is now in a phrase. No indirect object here.
“Emma read her father the letter.” → bare receiver. ‘Her father’ is the indirect object.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Does each sentence contain a bare indirect object, or is the receiver tucked inside a “to” or “for” phrase?

⚡ TRY IT · RECEIVER OR PHRASE?
Pick the correct answer for each.
1. Frank told Emma a secret.
2. Emma gave the picture to Harriet.
3. Mr. Weston sent Mrs. Weston an invitation.
4. Mrs. Elton bought a present for Jane.
▶  In your notebook, take this sentence and rewrite it both ways: “Mr. Knightley showed Emma the field.” Then click to check.
Bare receiver: “Mr. Knightley showed Emma the field.” (Emma is the indirect object.)   In a phrase: “Mr. Knightley showed the field to Emma.” (Emma is now inside a “to” phrase.) Same meaning; only the build changed.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

A bare receiver is an indirect object; a receiver behind to or for is not. Add your grammar practice to your Daily Practice notebook this week. Memory work: keep drilling passage 3, and review passages 1 and 2 so all three stay sharp.
