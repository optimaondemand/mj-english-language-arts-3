Lesson 1.16
Little Words That Show Position
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A small word can place one thing in relation to another.
A preposition is a word that shows how one thing relates to another in space, time, or direction: in, on, under, before, after, with, to, from, of, between. A preposition almost never travels alone. It points to a noun or pronoun called its object, and the preposition plus that object (plus any words in between) make a prepositional phrase. In “Emma walked to the village,” the preposition is to, its object is village, and the whole phrase is to the village. These phrases tell you where, when, and how, and Austen leans on them to set every scene.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to spot a preposition in a sentence.
2
I will be able to mark the whole prepositional phrase, from the preposition to its object.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which phrase tells you where something is? (No wrong answer.)

Here is how to find the phrase: locate the position word, then ask “to or with what?” The answer is the object, and everything from the preposition through the object is the phrase.

“Emma sat by the window.” → position word by, object window. Phrase: by the window.
“The letter came from Frank Churchill.” → position word from, object Frank Churchill. Phrase: from Frank Churchill.
“Jane Fairfax arrived in Highbury.” → position word in, object Highbury. Phrase: in Highbury.
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

Choose the prepositional phrase in each sentence.

1. Emma thought often of the visit.

3. Jane answered every question with great care.
4. Jane played beautifully on the pianoforte.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Prepositional phrases tell you where, when, and how. As you read this week, notice how Austen uses them to move characters around Highbury and to time their meetings. Memory work: drill all your memorized passages, both the opening sentence and “It was badly done, indeed!”
