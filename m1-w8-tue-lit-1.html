Lesson 1.37
Who Changes, and Who Stays the Same
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In a good novel, some people change and some do not. Both kinds do real work.
As Volume III nears its end, you can finally tell two kinds of characters apart. A dynamic character grows or changes in some important way by the end of the story: Emma learns to see her own faults. A static character stays essentially the same from start to finish: Mr. Woodhouse frets about health and dislikes change on the last page just as he did on the first. Static characters are not failures of writing. They give the story something steady to push against, and they often show us, by contrast, just how far the dynamic characters have come.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell a character who changes from one who stays the same.
2
I will be able to give a reason from the text for each sorting choice.

📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN COMPARE BEGINNINGS AND ENDINGS

Read Volume III, Chapters 7 to 12. As you read, hold each character against your memory of how they first appeared. Ask: is this person different now, or the same?

📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Austen often praises a character’s “candour.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
candour
Candour means openness and honesty; a habit of judging others kindly and speaking the plain truth. A character who gains candour has learned to be honest with herself, which is part of what makes Emma dynamic.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Candour comes from Latin candere, “to shine or glow white.” The same root lights up candle and candid, a very close cousin of candour itself.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing a character using candour. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“candour that lets her…”).
