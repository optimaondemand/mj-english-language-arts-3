📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
An author plants his concerns in the first pages, before he ever states them.
In the opening chapters, a quiet scholar named Ransom is walking through the English countryside when two men, Weston and Devine, seize him and carry him off in a spacecraft. C. S. Lewis was writing in the 1930s, worried about a particular idea of progress: that knowledge and power justify almost anything, including treating other people, and other worlds, as material to be used. Notice what Lewis lingers on. He cares about a person’s ordinary kindness, about the fear of the unknown, and about whether the men who reach for the stars have any reverence at all. An author does not always announce what he values. Often he shows it by what he slows down to describe and what questions he leaves hanging in the reader’s mind.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to infer what an author values from what he chooses to show.
2
I will be able to name a question the opening chapters raise.

📖 YOUR READING
Read Chapters 1 to 4 in your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet. This novel is under copyright, so the full text is not posted here. The syllabus lists the edition your class uses.
💬 ONE LINE TO WEIGH
Early on, Ransom is described as “a sedentary scholar.” (C. S. Lewis, Out of the Silent Planet, Ch. 1, quoted briefly for study.)
Lewis chooses to make his hero a reader and a walker, not a soldier or an inventor. Ask yourself why an author worried about reckless “progress” might want his hero to be exactly that kind of man.
⚡ PART 2 · WHAT DOES THE CHOICE SUGGEST?

For each authorial choice, pick the best reading of what Lewis seems to value or ask.

⚡ TRY IT · READ THE AUTHOR
What does each choice suggest?
1. Lewis makes Ransom a thoughtful scholar rather than a fighter.
2. Weston and Devine are willing to kidnap a man for their voyage.
3. Lewis dwells on Ransom’s dread of the unknown rather than rushing ahead.
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Lewis describes Ransom as a “sedentary” scholar. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
sedentary
Sedentary means spending much time seated; not active or moving about. A sedentary scholar is a man of the desk and the book. Lewis uses it to set up a contrast: this still, bookish man is about to be hurled across space.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Sedentary comes from Latin sedere, “to sit.” The same root sits inside session (a sitting, as of a court or class) and reside (to settle and stay).
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing Ransom using sedentary. Then rewrite it twice: once with a near synonym, once expanding it (“sedentary in a way that…”).
