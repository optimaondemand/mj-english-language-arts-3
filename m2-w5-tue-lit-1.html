Lesson 2.22
Listening to the Oyarsa: Sound and Alliteration
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How a passage sounds can carry as much meaning as what it says.
When the Oyarsa, the ruler of the planet, finally speaks to the men from Earth, Lewis does not just report the words. He shapes the rhythm and the sound of them so the speech feels calm, ancient, and weighty. One of his tools is alliteration: the repeating of the same beginning sound across nearby words, as in “silent stars” or “dark and deep.” Repeated sounds slow you down and make a line feel deliberate. As you read Chapters 17 to 20, read the Oyarsa’s words slowly enough to hear them. The sound is part of how Lewis makes this being seem wise rather than merely strange.

📝 PART 2 · WORD STUDY

The word for the sound pattern you are hunting is below. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
alliteration
Alliteration is the repeating of the same beginning consonant sound in two or more nearby words, such as “wild and windy” or “sun-warmed sand.” Writers use it to make a line memorable and to control its pace.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Alliteration comes from Latin ad- (“to”) and littera (“letter”): matching letters. The same root gives us literal (letter by letter) and literature (writing itself).
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a short phrase of your own that uses alliteration to describe a strange landscape. Then rewrite it twice: once changing the repeated sound, once stretching it into a full sentence you might use in your story.
⚡ PART 3 · MARK THE SOUND

Here is one short, attributed line from the Oyarsa’s speech, quoted so we can study its wording. Read it aloud first, then answer the questions about its sound.

“A bent hnau can do more evil than a broken one.”
C. S. Lewis, Out of the Silent Planet, Ch. 18 (short fair-use excerpt for study). Hnau is the word in the story for a rational, speaking creature.
⚡ TRY IT · HEAR THE PATTERN
Answer each question about the line above.
1. Which pair of words alliterates?
2. What does that sound pairing do for the meaning?
3. How does the sound match the speaker?
