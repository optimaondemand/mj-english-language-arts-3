Lesson 2.8
The Comma That Holds Two Thoughts Together
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When you join two complete thoughts, the comma goes before the joining word, not after.
On Monday you learned that a compound sentence joins two independent clauses as equals. The words that do the joining are seven small connectors. Their first letters spell FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so. The rule is simple and strict: when one of these words joins two complete thoughts, put a comma before it. So you write The hatch hissed, and the air turned cold, with the comma before and. Two common mistakes to avoid: do not drop the comma, and do not glue two complete thoughts together with only a comma and no joining word. That second error is called a comma splice, and Wednesday is the day to learn to spot it.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to place the comma correctly before a FANBOYS joining word.
2
I will be able to spot a comma splice and fix it.

📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Say both versions in your head. Which one is easier to read aloud? (No wrong answer.)

Here is the seven-word family, then the rule in action.

For   And   Nor   But   Or   Yet   So

Correct: “Ransom feared the worst, but he kept his voice steady.” (comma before but)
Wrong (comma splice): “Ransom feared the worst, he kept his voice steady.” (two complete thoughts, no joining word)
Watch out: “Ransom feared the worst and stayed silent.” needs no comma, because “stayed silent” is not a complete thought of its own.
