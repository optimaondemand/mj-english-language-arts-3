Lesson 1.4
Copying Austen’s Opening
Literature Lesson · Copywork
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You learn a great sentence best by writing it out in your own hand.
Copying a sentence by hand is not busywork. Your hand moves slowly enough for your eye to catch what fast reading skips: where the commas fall, how the parts balance, where the sentence turns. Today you will copy the opening sentence of Emma exactly, and look closely at how Austen builds it.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to copy Austen’s opening sentence by hand, with her exact punctuation.
2
I will be able to explain how Austen balances short parts to build one long sentence.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook, a pen or pencil, and your best handwriting.
✏️ PART 1 · COPY IT BY HAND
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Read the sentence below aloud once, pausing at every comma. How does reading it slowly feel? (No wrong answer.)

Copy this sentence into your notebook, once, in your best hand, with every comma exactly where Austen put it:

“Emma Woodhouse, handsome, clever, and rich, with a comfortable home and happy disposition, seemed to unite some of the best blessings of existence; and had lived nearly twenty-one years in the world with very little to distress or vex her.”
📤 DAILY PRACTICE · COMPLETION
Keep this in your writing notebook. At the end of the week you will photograph your notebook and turn it in as Week 1 Daily Practice, graded for completion: a careful, accurate copy.
📚 PART 2 · HOW THE SENTENCE IS BUILT

Austen’s sentence looks like one breath, but it is really a string of short, balanced parts hung on a simple spine: Emma Woodhouse…seemed to unite…the best blessings of existence. Each comma is a hinge that lets her add one more piece of praise. Then, at the very end, the tone shifts: “with very little to distress or vex her” sounds like good fortune, but it quietly plants the novel’s first trouble. A girl with nothing to trouble her may never learn to know herself.

⚡ TRY IT · NOTICE THE CRAFT
Look back at the sentence you copied.
1. Which part is a list of qualities describing Emma?
2. Which ending quietly hints at trouble to come?
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

The last word of the sentence is one worth keeping. Tap to check its meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
vex
To vex is to trouble, annoy, or irritate someone. Austen says almost nothing had ever vexed Emma, which is part of her problem.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Vex comes from Latin vexare, meaning “to shake, agitate, or trouble.” The same root survives in vexation, the noun for the state of being vexed.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Copy Austen’s phrase “little to distress or vex her.” Then write it twice in your own words: once keeping vex, once replacing it with a stronger or a gentler synonym. Notice how the feeling changes each time.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

You have copied a famous sentence and seen how its calm surface hides a warning. On Friday you will discuss what Emma does and does not yet know about herself. Memory work: you have written the opening sentence by hand and read it aloud all week. Try saying it once now with your eyes closed.
