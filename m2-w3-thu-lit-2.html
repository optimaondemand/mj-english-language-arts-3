Lesson 2.14
Who Is Looking Changes What We See
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The same event looks like a different event depending on who is watching it.
Point of view is the position from which a story is told and the eyes through which we see its world. For most of the novel we see Malacandra through Ransom, a human, so what is normal to the hrossa seems strange to us. But the trick works the other way too. To the hrossa, it is Ransom who is the odd one: small habits of ours that we never question would look foreign, even troubling, to them. When Lewis lets us imagine Earth and humanity through Malacandran eyes, the central question of this module presses on us: what do we miss when we assume our own way of seeing is simply the right one?
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain how point of view shapes what a reader notices and judges.
2
I will be able to rewrite one human moment as a Malacandran might see it.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet, Chapters 9 to 12, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN SWITCH SIDES
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Imagine explaining money to someone who has never used it. Which feeling comes first? (No wrong answer.)

Read Chapters 9 to 12 in your own copy. Look for moments where Ransom tries to explain something human to a hross, or where a hross reacts to a human idea in a way that surprises Ransom. Those are the seams where two points of view meet.

📖 YOUR READING
Read Chapters 9 to 12 in your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet. The novel is under copyright, so the text is not reprinted here; keep your book beside you.
⚡ PART 2 · WHOSE EYES?

Each line below describes the same world. Decide whose point of view it comes from: a human seeing Malacandra, or a hross seeing a human.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE POINT OF VIEW
Whose point of view does each line show?
1. “The tall water creatures should have been monsters, yet they moved with strange grace.”
2. “The visitor gathered far more than it could eat, and seemed afraid to share.”
3. “The small creature spoke very fast and seemed troubled, though nothing was chasing it.”
4. “Their deep voices rose and fell in a way no choir at home would ever attempt.”
📝 PART 3 · REWRITE ONE MOMENT

Now you try the switch yourself. Pick one ordinary human thing: a wristwatch, a handful of coins, a crowded city street, or a person hurrying to be on time. Write three or four sentences describing it as a hross might, a creature who has never seen it and does not share our reasons for it. Commit your version first, then open the example.

▶  Write your Malacandran-eyes version in your notebook first. Then click to see one example.
Example (a wristwatch): “The creature wore a small round eye on its arm and looked into it often, as if asking permission. When the eye showed a certain mark, the creature grew anxious and hurried, though no one had called it and nothing was wrong. We could not understand why it let so small a thing command it.” Your version will differ; the test is whether you describe the thing without using our names or our reasons for it.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Point of view is not just a technique; in this novel it is the theme. Seeing ourselves through another’s eyes is how Lewis asks us to question what we take for granted. Carry that into your own story, where your reader meets your world through one chosen pair of eyes. Memory work: keep working on Drill passage 1.
