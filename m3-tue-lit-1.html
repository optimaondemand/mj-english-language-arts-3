📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A poem is meant to be heard, not just seen.
Walt Whitman published “When I Heard the Learn’d Astronomer” in 1865. It is one short sentence, eight lines long. A speaker sits in a lecture hall while an expert explains the stars with proofs, figures, charts, and diagrams. Halfway through, the speaker grows tired and sick of it all, rises, and walks out into the night, where he simply looks up at the stars in silence. The poem holds two ways of meeting the same sky: measuring it, and wondering at it. Today you will hear it read aloud, read it yourself, and find the exact place where it turns from one way to the other.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to read the whole poem aloud and follow its single long sentence.
2
I will be able to point to the line where the poem turns from the lecture hall to the night.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
The poem (printed below), your writing notebook, and a quiet place to read aloud.
📖 PART 1 · LISTEN, THEN READ ALOUD
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a time someone explained something to you in great detail. How did it leave you feeling? (No wrong answer.)
🔊 LISTEN FIRST
Listen to the poem read aloud once before you read it yourself. [Teacher: embed an approved read-aloud recording here.]

Now read the whole poem aloud, slowly. It is one long sentence, so let your voice carry through the line breaks and only fully stop at the end.

WHEN I HEARD THE LEARN’D ASTRONOMER · WALT WHITMAN (1865)
When I heard the learn’d astronomer,
When the proofs, the figures, were ranged in columns before me,
When I was shown the charts and diagrams, to add, divide, and measure them,
When I sitting heard the astronomer where he lectured with much applause in the lecture-room,
How soon unaccountable I became tired and sick,
Till rising and gliding out I wander’d off by myself,
In the mystical moist night-air, and from time to time,
Look’d up in perfect silence at the stars.
This poem is in the public domain.
📝 PART 2 · WORD STUDY

Whitman calls the night-air “mystical.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
mystical
Mystical means having a meaning beyond what reason alone can explain; touched with mystery or wonder. By calling the night-air mystical, Whitman sets the wonder of the stars against the measuring done inside the lecture hall.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Mystical comes from Greek myein, “to close the eyes or lips,” the verb behind the secret rites of the ancient mystery religions. The same root gives us mystery and mysterious, both carrying that old sense of something deliberately hidden from ordinary sight.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence using mystical. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“mystical in the sense that…”).
⚡ PART 3 · FIND THE TURN

The poem starts in the lecture hall and ends under the stars. Answer each about where and how it turns.

⚡ TRY IT · WHERE DOES IT TURN?
Use the printed poem above to answer.
1. At which line does the poem turn away from the lecture?
2. What fills the first four lines?
3. How does the speaker meet the stars at the end?
▶  In your notebook, copy the one line where you feel the poem turn, and write one sentence on why it feels like the turn. Click to compare.
Many readers mark line five, “How soon unaccountable I became tired and sick.” It is the first line about the speaker’s own feeling rather than the lecture, and everything after it moves outdoors and quiets down. Your reasoning may differ; what matters is that you can point to a line and say why.
