Lesson 1.32
When Things Are Given a Life of Their Own
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Sometimes a writer hands a feeling, an object, or an idea the power to act like a person.
When a writer gives a human action or feeling to something that is not human, the writer is using personification. The wind whispers; a clock scolds; hope flatters us. Austen uses this quietly. She lets weather, time, and even feelings behave like people who push her characters around. Watching for it tells you where the pressure in a scene is really coming from. As you read Volume III, Chapters 1 to 6, your job is to find three places where something not human is given a human action, and to name what each one stands for. Naming what a personification represents is half the skill: the wind is just wind until you ask what it is doing to the people in the room.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to spot a place where a non-human thing is given a human action or feeling.
2
I will be able to name what each personification stands for in the scene.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your copy of Emma (or the linked PDF), Vol. III, Ch. 1–6, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN WATCH FOR IT
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a phrase you have heard where something not alive seems to act. What was it doing? (No wrong answer.)

Read Volume III, Chapters 1 to 6. Each time something not human seems to act or feel, pause and ask two questions: what is the human action, and what does that thing stand for here?

📖 YOUR READING
Emma full text (PDF), Vol. III, Ch. 1–6  [Teacher: confirm the Canvas file link on deploy.]

Here is a worked example so you know what you are hunting for.

“Her spirits sank.”
Human action: sank, as if spirits could drop like a weight in water.
What it stands for: Emma’s mood and hope, suddenly heavier than she can lift.
⚡ PART 2 · NAME WHAT IT STANDS FOR

In each line, something not human is given a human action. Choose what that thing stands for in the scene.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT IS BEING PERSONIFIED?
What does the personified thing stand for?
1. “Hope flattered her into believing she had been right.”
2. “The morning frowned over Highbury.”
3. “The truth refused to leave her in peace.”
4. “The evening crept on, slow and unwilling.”

These lines are written to practice the skill. In your notebook, find three real examples from your reading of Chapters 1 to 6, and for each one write the line, the human action, and what it stands for.

📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Austen often calls a remark or a manner “arch.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
arch
Arch (as an adjective) means playfully teasing or sly, with a knowing little smile behind the words. An arch remark hints at more than it says. Frank Churchill, in these chapters, often speaks in an arch way that keeps Emma guessing.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Arch comes from Greek archi-, meaning “chief” or “first,” the same root you can hear in monarch (one chief ruler) and architect (a chief builder). Austen borrows that “chief” sense sideways: an arch remark acts as though it were the most knowing thing in the room, with a sly smile behind it.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing a character using arch. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“arch in a way that…”).
