📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Not every source that turns up in a search is equally trustworthy, and knowing the difference is a skill you can learn.
Your Week 10 essay on Emma will ask you to bring in an outside source. Before you can use one well, you need to know where to look, what kind of source you are holding, and how to tell whether it deserves your trust. That is today's work.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what a research database is and why a writer might use one instead of a general web search.
2
I will be able to tell a primary source from a secondary source, and judge whether a given source is credible.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
A device with internet access, if you have one (optional for this lesson), your Reading Journal and Essay Organizer, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · WHAT A RESEARCH DATABASE IS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think about the last time you looked something up for a school project. Where did you search first? (No wrong answer.)

When you search for information online, a general search engine hands you everything at once: news, opinions, encyclopedia pages, advertising, and real scholarship, all mixed together with no promise that any of it has been checked. A research database works differently. It collects material that has already been selected, often reviewed by other experts in the field, and organized so you can search by subject, author, or date. JSTOR is one real example: it is an academic database that many school and public libraries provide access to, though it is not something everyone can reach for free on their own, so check with your school or public librarian about what your library card unlocks. If your library does not offer JSTOR specifically, its own catalog is still a strong place to start, since a librarian has already sorted much of that collection by subject and reliability for you.

💡 WHY IT MATTERS
A database will not do your thinking for you, but it narrows the field to material that has already passed some basic tests of quality. That saves you from having to make every judgment about every source from scratch.
⚡ PART 2 · PRIMARY OR SECONDARY?

Sources come in two basic kinds. A primary source is firsthand: a document, object, or account produced at the time and place you are studying, by someone who was actually there. A secondary source stands at one remove: it is someone later analyzing, explaining, or arguing about the primary material. Emma itself is a primary source for early nineteenth-century English social life: Jane Austen wrote it in 1815, drawing on the manners, conversation, and courtship customs of her own world, so the novel is direct evidence of how people of that time and place talked and behaved. A scholarly article written by a literary critic decades later, analyzing how Austen uses irony to expose Emma’s blind spots, is a secondary source: its subject is the novel, not the century itself.

⚡ TRY IT · PRIMARY OR SECONDARY?
Sort each source below by what kind of evidence it is.
1. A letter Jane Austen wrote to her sister Cassandra in 1814, describing a dance she attended.
2. A peer-reviewed journal article comparing courtship customs across Austen’s six novels.
3. Emma, the 1815 novel itself.
4. An encyclopedia entry, written recently, summarizing the plot and major themes of Emma.
5. A newspaper advertisement from 1816 for a dance held in an English country town.
📝 PART 3 · IS THIS SOURCE CREDIBLE?

Not everything that looks official is trustworthy, and not everything that looks casual is worthless, so you need a few real questions to ask of any source. First, who wrote it, and do they have real expertise on this subject? Second, who published it: a respected journal or press, a museum, a university, or an anonymous website with no editor? Third, when was it written or last updated, since some subjects change fast and a source can go out of date? Fourth, and most important, is the source reporting a fact you could check elsewhere, or is it mostly one person’s opinion dressed up as fact? A source can fail on one of these points and still be worth using carefully, but a source that fails on all four is not one to build an argument on.

▶  Before you look, decide for yourself: would you trust a blog post with no named author, last updated years ago, that mixes the writer's opinions in with historical claims about Austen's England? Write yes or no with your reasoning in your notebook. Then click to check.
Probably not, at least not on its own. No named author means you cannot check anyone’s expertise; an old update date is a problem for some subjects and not others; and mixing opinion with fact, without marking the difference, makes it hard to know which claims to trust. That does not mean the post is worthless; it can still be a good place to find a lead. But you would want to verify any specific claim against a more credible source before you used it in your own writing.
