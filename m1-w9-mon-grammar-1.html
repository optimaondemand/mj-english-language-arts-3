Lesson 1.41
Commas That Separate and Join
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A comma in the right place keeps a reader from getting lost.
You are about to write an essay, so your commas matter. Three of the most common comma jobs are worth nailing down today. First, a comma separates items in a series: Emma is clever, kind, and a little proud. Second, a comma plus a joining word (and, but, or, so, yet) connects two complete sentences into one: Emma plans the match, but Mr. Knightley disapproves. Third, a comma follows a short opener that comes before the main sentence: After her mistake, Emma reflects. Get these three right and your sentences will read cleanly.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to place commas correctly in a series of three or more items.
2
I will be able to use a comma plus a joining word to connect two complete sentences.
3
I will be able to put a comma after an opener at the start of a sentence.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which of these reads more clearly to you? (No wrong answer.)

Here are the three jobs side by side. Watch where each comma falls and why.

Series: “Highbury offers her comfort, company, and influence.” → commas between three items. Separate.
Two sentences joined: “Emma means well, but she misreads Harriet.” → comma before the joining word. Join.
Opener: “By the end of the novel, Emma sees herself clearly.” → comma after the opening phrase. Open.

A quick test for the second job: if the words on both sides of the joining word could each stand alone as a sentence, you usually need the comma.

⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

For each sentence, choose the comma job it needs.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH COMMA JOB?
What is the comma doing (or what is missing)?
1. Emma had arranged everything ___ so she expected to be thanked.
2. She is generous ___ lively ___ and devoted to her father.
3. After the harvest dinner ___ Emma finally apologized.
4. They walked ___ and talked all the way to Donwell.
▶  In your notebook, write one sentence with a series, one that joins two sentences, and one with an opener. Then click to compare.
Series: “Emma plans, hopes, and meddles.”   Joined: “She apologized, and Mr. Knightley forgave her.”   Opener: “In the end, she chose honesty.” Your sentences will differ; check that each comma is doing one of the three jobs.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

You will reach for all three of these jobs in your essay this week. Separate items in a series, join two sentences with a comma plus a joining word, and set off your openers. Memory work: drill all three of your passages so they are ready to quote in the essay.
