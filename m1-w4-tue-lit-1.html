Lesson 1.17
Telling What Happens, In Order
Literature Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Before you can judge a scene, you have to be able to say what happened in it.
Volume II opens a new season in Highbury. Jane Fairfax, quiet and accomplished, comes to stay with her aunt, and the much-talked-of Frank Churchill finally arrives. Emma is curious about both. Today you will practice telling what happens in your own words, in the order it happens. A good retelling keeps the events in sequence, names who did what, and leaves out your opinion for now. That ordered account becomes the ground you will stand on when, later, you argue what a scene means.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to retell the main events of the chapters in the order they happen.
2
I will be able to tell the difference between what happened and what I think about it.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your copy of Emma (or the linked PDF), Vol. II, Ch. 1–6, and your writing notebook.
📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN RETELL
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of telling a friend the plot of a movie. What do you keep, and what do you leave out? (No wrong answer.)

Read Volume II, Chapters 1 to 6. As you read, keep a short list in your notebook: who arrives, who visits whom, and in what order. You are gathering the facts of the build-up toward the famous summer outing to Box Hill.

📖 YOUR READING
Emma: full text (PDF), Vol. II, Ch. 1–6  [Teacher: confirm the Canvas file link on deploy.]

Here is a model retelling of one earlier event, told plainly and in order:

First, Mr. Weston tells the neighborhood that his son will visit. Then the visit is delayed. Finally, Frank Churchill arrives in Highbury and calls on the Westons. Notice: events in order, no opinion yet.
⚡ PART 2 · EVENT, OR OPINION?

A good retelling reports events. For each statement, decide whether it states what happened or gives an opinion about it.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT HAPPENED VS. WHAT I THINK
Is each statement an event or an opinion?
1. Frank Churchill arrived in Highbury and called on the Westons.
2. Frank is far too charming for his own good.
3. Jane Fairfax came to stay with her aunt and grandmother.
4. Emma’s coolness toward Jane is unfair.
▶  In your notebook, write a 4 to 6 sentence retelling of the chapters: events only, in order. Then click for a checklist.
Check your retelling: Does it name who did what? Are the events in order? Did you keep your opinions out for now? (You will get to your opinions in the discussion and the project. Today, just tell the story.)
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Austen describes Frank’s eager helpfulness as “officious.” Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
officious
Officious means too eager to offer help or advice that no one asked for. It sounds polite but carries a warning: a person can be busy on your behalf in a way that serves himself.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Officious traces to Latin officium, meaning “duty” or “service.” The same root gives us office and official, both about proper duty; officious describes someone who takes on duty nobody assigned.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing Frank using officious. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“officious in a way that…”).

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

A clear retelling is the foundation of clear thinking. Once you can say plainly what happened between Emma, Frank, and Jane, you can begin to ask what it means. Memory work: drill all your memorized passages, both the opening sentence and “It was badly done, indeed!”
