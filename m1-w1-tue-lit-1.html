Who Is Emma Woodhouse?
Literature Lesson · Logic Stage
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What do we learn about a person from the very first thing we are told about her?
In the opening chapters of Emma, Jane Austen introduces a heroine she calls “handsome, clever, and rich,” and then spends an entire novel showing us the difference between what Emma believes about herself and what is true. Today you meet the people of Highbury and start watching how Austen reveals character not by telling us a person’s flaws, but by showing what they do and how others respond.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to identify the main characters of Highbury and the household each belongs to.
2
I will be able to retell the events of Chapter 1 accurately, in my own words.

📚 PART 1 · THE PEOPLE OF HIGHBURY
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of someone who is generous and well-liked but almost always certain they are right. What tends to happen when a person like that decides to “help”? (No wrong answer.)

Four people, three houses

Emma Woodhouse lives at Hartfield with her gentle, anxious father, Mr. Woodhouse, who is frightened of any change at all. As the novel opens, Emma’s former governess and dear friend, Miss Taylor, has just married Mr. Weston and moved to Randalls, leaving Emma without her closest companion and a little too pleased with herself for having “made” the match. Nearby at Donwell Abbey lives Mr. Knightley, an old family friend who is, as Austen says, “one of the few people who could see faults in Emma Woodhouse, and the only one who ever told her of them.” Notice that Austen never simply announces that Emma is vain; she lets it surface in what Emma does and in how Mr. Knightley answers her.

📖 PART 2 · READ, THEN RETELL

Read Volume I, Chapters 1–3 in one sitting if you can: one reading, straight through, with no stopping to look things up. Then, in your writing notebook, write a short paragraph retelling what happens in Chapter 1 in your own words and in the order it occurs. This is narration: not a summary you copy, but the events as your own mind has received them. Your notebook work is collected at the end of the week in Week 1 Daily Practice.

📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Austen writes that Emma has a “happy disposition.” Tap the card to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
disposition
A person’s usual temper or mood; the way they tend to feel and behave. Austen’s “happy disposition” tells us Emma is naturally cheerful.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Disposition comes from Latin dis- (“apart”) and ponere (“to place, to put”): literally how something is put together. The same root ponere turns up in expose, component, and position.
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Copy Austen’s phrase, then rewrite it twice about someone you know: once swapping in a synonym for disposition, once expanding the phrase (“a happy disposition that…”). Notice how the meaning shifts each time.
