Lesson 1.6
Verbs That Act, Verbs That Link
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Some verbs do something. Others simply connect.
Last week you found the verb in a sentence. Verbs come in two kinds. An action verb shows something happening: Emma walks, Harriet blushes, Mr. Knightley argues. A linking verb does not show action at all; it links the subject to a word that renames or describes it: Emma is clever, Harriet seems shy. The most common linking verb is to be (is, am, are, was, were), along with a few others like seem, become, feel, and look. Telling them apart helps you see whether a sentence is showing you an action or making a claim about someone.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell an action verb from a linking verb.
2
I will be able to name the word a linking verb connects to the subject.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5–10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Which of these feels like it shows an action? (No wrong answer.)

Watch the test for a linking verb: if you can replace the verb with is or was and the sentence still makes sense, it is probably linking.

“Harriet blushed at the compliment.” → something happens. Action verb.
“Harriet seemed pleased.” → no action; “seemed” links Harriet to “pleased.” Linking verb.
“Mr. Knightley is honest.” → “is” links Mr. Knightley to “honest.” Linking verb.

▶  In your notebook, write one sentence about a character with an action verb and one with a linking verb. Then click to see examples.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Action verbs show; linking verbs describe. As you read this week, notice how often Austen tells you what a character is rather than what they do. Memory work: keep the opening sentence sharp, and start learning “It was badly done, indeed!”
