Lesson 1.43
Commas Around Extra Information
Grammar Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Some commas come in pairs, like the two ends of a pause.
Yesterday you separated, joined, and opened. Today you wrap commas around extra information. When a phrase adds a detail the sentence could survive without, you fence it off with a comma on each side: Mr. Knightley, a man of sense, tells Emma the truth. Remove the fenced part and the sentence still stands: Mr. Knightley tells Emma the truth. The same pairing handles a date inside a sentence (On October 12, 1815, the book appeared) and a place inside an address (She lived in Highbury, Surrey, all her life). The test is simple: if the detail is just extra, fence it with commas.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to fence off a renaming phrase or an extra detail with a pair of commas.
2
I will be able to tell extra information from information the sentence needs.
3
I will be able to punctuate dates and addresses inside a sentence.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your writing notebook and a pencil. About 5 to 10 minutes of focused practice.
📚 PART 1 · A WORKED EXAMPLE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you drop the underlined part, does the sentence still make sense? “Emma, the heroine of the novel, lives in Highbury.” (No wrong answer.)

Watch the difference between extra information and necessary information.

Extra: “Mr. Woodhouse, Emma’s anxious father, dislikes change.” → drop the middle and it still stands. Fence it.
Necessary: “The man who corrects Emma is Mr. Knightley.” → drop “who corrects Emma” and you lose the meaning. No commas.
Date / address: “The novel, published in December 1815, was set in Surrey, England.” Fence the extras.
▶  In your notebook, add commas to this sentence, then click to check: “Harriet a sweet girl trusted Emma completely.”
Answer: “Harriet, a sweet girl, trusted Emma completely.”   “A sweet girl” renames Harriet and is extra, so a comma fences each end. Read it without the middle to confirm: “Harriet trusted Emma completely.”
⚡ PART 2 · YOUR TURN

For each sentence, decide whether the marked part needs a pair of commas around it.

⚡ TRY IT · FENCE IT OR LEAVE IT?
Does the underlined part need commas around it?
1. Mrs. Weston, Emma’s old governess, had married and moved away.
2. The match that Emma arranged for Harriet failed completely.
3. The story closes in October 1815 with a wedding at Highbury.
4. She had spent her whole life in Highbury Surrey England.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

When you introduce a character or a source in your essay, you will lean on these comma pairs. Fence the extra, leave the necessary alone. Memory work: drill all three passages; aim to say each one without a single stumble.
