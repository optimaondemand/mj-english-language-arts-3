Spotting the Reasoning: Two Ways an Argument Goes Wrong
A short, ungraded page. It sits alongside this week’s grammar and Word Study, not in place of them.
📖 WHY THIS MATTERS
Emma Woodhouse is confident. She is also, this week, wrong about several things, and it is worth asking exactly how her thinking goes wrong. Two of her habits of mind have names, and once you know the names, you will start noticing the same two moves everywhere: in ads, in arguments with friends, in your own snap judgments. Below, each one gets a plain definition, then a chance to spot it in this week’s reading.
⚡ ONE · HASTY GENERALIZATION
A hasty generalization is a big conclusion built on too little evidence, often just a first impression. You meet someone for five minutes and decide you know exactly what kind of person they are for good. It feels like a discovery. It is really a guess wearing a fact’s clothing.
Emma meets Harriet Smith, likes her pretty face and her agreeable, easily-led manner, and within a few pages has decided Harriet must be “the natural daughter of a gentleman,” with birth and prospects to match. Emma has no actual information about Harriet’s parents. She has a smile and a manner, and from that alone she builds a whole family history.
⚡ TRY IT · HASTY GENERALIZATION, OR A FAIR CONCLUSION?
For each moment from this week’s chapters, decide: is the thinking a hasty generalization, or a fair conclusion built on real evidence?
1. Emma meets Harriet only a few times, notices her pretty face and pleasing manner, and decides she must be the well-born daughter of a gentleman.
2. Mr. Knightley has watched Robert Martin manage his farm carefully for several years, and on that record judges him a sensible, respectable man.
3. After Harriet answers one question timidly, Emma privately decides Harriet has nothing worth saying on any subject.
⚡ TWO · CIRCULAR REASONING
Circular reasoning happens when someone tries to prove a claim by using that very same claim as the proof. The argument does not go anywhere; it just walks in a circle back to where it started. It can sound confident and even sound like an answer, but repeating a claim is not the same as backing it up.
Watch Emma’s logic about matching Harriet with Mr. Elton. The match is good, she tells herself, because she is the one who thought of it, and she only ever thinks of good matches. Ask where the outside evidence is, evidence that does not just come back to Emma’s own opinion of herself, and there is none. The claim is proving itself.
⚡ TRY IT · CIRCULAR, OR A REAL REASON?
For each moment, decide: does the reasoning circle back on itself, or does it point to something outside the claim?
1. Emma is sure the match between Harriet and Mr. Elton is a good idea, because she is the one who thought of it, and she only ever comes up with good ideas.
2. Emma notices that Mr. Elton visits Hartfield often, praises Harriet’s drawings, and asks after her health nearly every time he calls.
3. When Harriet wonders whether she is really too good for Robert Martin, Emma answers that of course she is, since Emma would never have encouraged the idea unless it were true.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Naming a habit of thought is the first step toward catching it in your own reasoning, not just Emma’s. As you keep reading this module, watch for other moments where a character is sure of something, and ask what is actually holding that certainty up.
