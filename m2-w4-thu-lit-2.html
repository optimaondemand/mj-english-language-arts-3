📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A good writer makes you feel the strangeness of a place and worry about what comes next.
As Ransom journeys toward Meldilorn, Lewis does two things at once. He uses imagery, language that appeals to the senses, to make an alien world feel real: the color of the rock, the thin cold air of the high places, the strange shapes on the horizon. And he builds suspense, the feeling that something important is about to happen, by holding back what waits at the end of the road. We do not yet know what the Oyarsa is, and that not-knowing pulls us forward. Today you will watch how Lewis keeps wonder alive, then carry those same tools into the draft of your own story.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to identify imagery and suspense in the journey to Meldilorn.
2
I will be able to use imagery and held-back information to draft my own story.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Your own copy of Out of the Silent Planet, Chapters 13 to 16, your writing notebook, and your story outline from Week 3.
📖 PART 1 · READ, THEN NARRATE
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a time you were heading somewhere unknown. What made the trip feel tense or exciting? (No wrong answer.)

Reread the journey to Meldilorn in Chapters 13 to 16 of your own copy. Then, in your notebook, narrate it back in your own words: in five or six sentences, tell what happens on Ransom’s journey, in order. Use your own phrasing, not the book’s.

📖 YOUR READING
Reread the journey in Chapters 13 to 16 of your own copy. This novel is under copyright, so the text is not reprinted here.
⚡ PART 2 · IMAGERY OR SUSPENSE?

A writer can make a moment vivid (imagery) or make us anxious about what comes next (suspense). For each line, choose which the writer is mainly doing. These lines are written for practice, not quoted from the book.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT IS THE WRITER DOING?
Imagery or suspense?
1. The air grew thin and cold, and the pale rock glowed under a small, distant sun.
2. Whatever waited at the end of the valley, no one would name it to him.
3. Blue-green water lapped the shore, and the sharp light caught the smell of strange weed.
4. A low sound followed him for an hour, always behind, always nearer.
📝 PART 3 · WORD STUDY

Meldilorn is full of vivid sights. Tap to check the meaning, then practice the word several ways in your Organizer.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
vivid
Vivid means so bright, clear, and detailed that it seems to come alive in your mind. Vivid imagery makes a reader feel they are standing in the scene.
🌱 WORD ROOTS
Vivid comes from Latin vivere, “to live.” The same root lives on in vivacious (full of life) and revive (to bring back to life).
📝 WORD STUDY · IN YOUR ORGANIZER
Write a sentence describing a place using vivid. Then rewrite it twice: once with a synonym, once expanding it (“vivid enough that…”).
✏️ PART 4 · DRAFTING GUIDANCE FOR YOUR STORY

This is your week to write the whole first-contact story. Borrow Lewis’s two tools as you go.

1
Start from your outline. Use the outline you built in Week 3. You already know who arrives, where, and who they meet.
2
Use imagery to make the world real. Give your reader at least three concrete senses in the opening: a color, a sound, a smell or texture that belongs only to your world.
3
Hold something back. Do not explain the strangest thing right away. Let your narrator and your reader wonder, the way Lewis keeps the Oyarsa a mystery until the end of the road.
▶  Draft one opening sentence for your story that uses a concrete sense detail. Then click to see a sample opening.
“The hatch hissed open, and the first thing I noticed was the smell: hot copper, and under it something sweet I had no name for.” Notice the two senses and the held-back mystery (“something sweet I had no name for”). Your own opening will differ; aim for a real sense detail in your first two lines.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Imagery makes a world real; suspense makes a reader stay. You now have both tools and an outline, so this week you write the whole draft. Memory work: keep working on Drill passage 1.
